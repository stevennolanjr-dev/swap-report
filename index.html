War on the Rocks · May 7, 2026
The U.S. can wargame munitions consumption but cannot model acquisition policy

The recurring WOTR thesis: high-fidelity consumption models exist; counterfactual acquisition models do not. The U.S. has the data on what current policy delivers to the field but no equivalent model for how alternative acquisition policies would have changed pre-war stockpiles. Acquisition reform needs its own wargame.

SWAP's Take

Methodologically right and politically inconvenient. Acquisition wargaming is harder than campaign wargaming because the counterfactuals stretch over ten- to fifteen-year horizons and the political-economy variables (industrial base, supplier consolidation, rare-earth dependency) are not native to the campaign-wargame community. The institutional home for the work is probably CAPE or RAND PAF rather than the war colleges. That itself is the political problem: acquisition reform cannot be wargamed in the same room as the appropriations decision it is supposed to inform.

Saturday read. Three pieces, one diagnosis. The U.S. defense industrial base cannot meet current operational tempo, the institutional response is converging on emergency authorities plus new acquisition rules, and the methodological tools needed to evaluate the response are themselves missing. The FY27 cycle is the consequential acquisition fight in two decades; the deep-source desk this week is the leading indicator.
