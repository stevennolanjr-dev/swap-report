How It Aged
PARTIAL NK Launch Cadence Test: Sub-Launched System by April 30 (filed April 19)

The April 19 claim: North Korea’s cadence of four launches in 12 days would yield a fifth by April 30; if it included a sub-launched platform, it would confirm that Pyongyang was exploiting the Iran distraction to test capabilities they could not otherwise. INDOPACOM called it a “departure from normal testing rhythm.”

What happened: USNI News confirmed a second cluster-warhead Hwasong-11 test on April 24, six days after the first. The cadence claim holds. No sub-launched system materialized by today’s deadline. The diagnostic was range and platform; the second test repeated the same Hwasong-11 at the same short range. That puts this closer to capability validation on the Korean Peninsula target set than distraction exploitation.

Lesson: The cadence signal was real and was confirmed. The sub-launch upgrade test was the speculative element. Two cluster-warhead tests in six days is doctrine development, not a showcase; the third test will clarify the target-set ambition more than this one did.

BUSTED Islamabad Proximity Talks: “Mutual Restraint” Language by April 27 (filed April 25)

The claim: the proximity-talks architecture (Iranian and U.S. delegations in separate Islamabad facilities, Pakistani FM shuttling) would produce a Pakistani joint statement by April 27 using “mutual steps,” “sequenced measures,” or equivalent face-saving language; both sides had converged on structure and were negotiating the announcement, not the deal.

What happened: Bloomberg headlined “US-Iran Talks Stall, Highlighting Limits of Pakistan’s Mediation Drive.” No joint statement. No structural language. The U.S. envoys did not travel to Islamabad. Iran’s proposal to reopen Hormuz in exchange for blockade suspension was delivered through Pakistan and immediately rejected by Rubio as unacceptable. Pakistan’s mediation window has likely closed for this cycle.

Lesson: The convergence-by-choreography thesis breaks down when one party (in this case the U.S.) has no domestic political need to close. The administration gains nothing from a sequenced announcement that Iran can characterize as a concession. The Oslo and Camp David II parallels required a president under electoral pressure to show progress. That condition is absent here. Mediation architecture is necessary but not sufficient; the principals’ political calendars drive the timing, not the venue.

War on the Rocks
Correcting Course in the Indo-Pacific
The piece examines whether the Iran war’s resource demands and strategic distraction have created an irreversible gap in Indo-Pacific deterrence posture, arguing that the administration needs a deliberate rebalancing rather than assuming the Iran and Indo-Pacific theaters can be managed in parallel at current operational tempo. It addresses munitions depletion, forward-presence signaling, and the alliance credibility question that China is watching most closely.
SWAP’s Take

The piece’s framing is honest about the trade-off the administration has been unwilling to name publicly: every Tomahawk expended at Hormuz is a Tomahawk not available in the South China Sea. The argument is structurally sound. What the piece understates is the Chinese perspective on Iran as a data-collection gift: 62 days of live-fire U.S. blockade enforcement is providing PLA Navy planners with exactly the operational intelligence they need for Taiwan Strait contingency planning, covering ROE, enforcement-zone mechanics, and how U.S.-flag and allied-flag commercial shipping behaves under naval pressure. The “deterrence gap” framing treats the problem as resource allocation. The more precise framing is that China is learning from the gap while the administration fills it.

MWI at West Point
Hitting Where It Hurts: Deep Strikes, Oil Infrastructure, and Kyiv’s Theory of Victory
The piece examines Ukraine’s sustained campaign against Russian oil processing infrastructure, analyzing whether targeting energy production imposes the economic cost on Moscow that prior infrastructure targeting (power grid, logistics) failed to achieve. It distinguishes between the political-economic logic of refinery strikes and the precision-dependent theory of victory that Kyiv is attempting to validate under severe munitions constraints.
SWAP’s Take

This piece arrives on the same day that environmental disaster fears from Russian refinery strikes are dominating Al Jazeera coverage of Ukraine. That timing is analytically useful: the theory of victory in oil infrastructure targeting requires inflicting economic pain faster than the targeting produces international sympathy for the target. The Battle of Britain analogy is worth invoking carefully here. German bombing of British industrial targets steeled British public resolve rather than breaking it. The question for Ukraine’s refinery campaign is whether Russian public opinion operates differently under authoritarian suppression, and whether oil revenue disruption feeds through to war-sustaining capacity quickly enough to matter. The piece is honest that the jury is still out on both conditions.

Source may be paywalled.
SWAP’s Take

The Dayan eulogy is one of the most honest strategic statements any Israeli leader ever made publicly, and the piece is right that it has been inconvenient to remember. The argument lands best in its diagnosis: Israel has conflated security with dominance in a way that Dayan explicitly warned against. Where it thins is the prescription gap. The piece demonstrates the problem clearly but does not engage seriously with the absence of a viable political interlocutor on the Palestinian side, the Gulf normalization dynamic that has been permanently altered by the Iran war, or the domestic Israeli political constraints that would make any “live with neighbors” strategic reset impossible under the current coalition. Cite it for the historical grounding; build your own prescription.

Small Wars Journal
Sabotage from Afar: How Undeclared Drone Armies Prolong War and Derail Peace
The piece examines how autonomous drone systems operated by state and non-state actors below the threshold of declared conflict are becoming the primary tool for sabotaging diplomatic progress. Case studies include IRGC maritime drones, Houthi Red Sea campaigns, and Ukrainian commercial-drone-converted battlefield systems. The central argument: undeclared drone wars are structurally incompatible with ceasefire architecture because attribution is deniable and enforcement is impossible at the unit level.
SWAP’s Take

The timing of this piece is good. The IRGC’s continued harassment of commercial shipping in Hormuz during the ceasefire is exactly the pattern the piece describes: deniable, below-threshold, structurally destructive to the diplomatic track but not independently casus belli for resuming strikes. The piece’s analytical contribution is framing this not as a tactical problem (attribution, ROE) but as an architectural one: modern ceasefire agreements were designed before drones made below-threshold coercion scalable and sustainable. The implication for Hormuz specifically is that IRGC drone harassment can continue indefinitely during any negotiated ceasefire unless the agreement explicitly addresses enforcement mechanisms for exactly this category of action.

The piece is analytically well-grounded in the Arreguín-Toft tradition, and the Iran application is apt: the regime’s survival at day 62, even in degraded form, is itself a form of strategic success if the relevant audience is domestic Iranian legitimacy rather than military capability. But the piece’s framework has a blind spot. The “weak becomes strong” thesis requires the weak party to survive long enough for the strong party’s political will to erode. The IRGC’s economic fragmentation and the succession crisis create a different variable: a regime that can win the asymmetric war in theory but may not survive the peace. Iran’s leadership problem is not that it cannot outlast the blockade; it is that outlasting the blockade may not produce a regime capable of governing the aftermath.
