BLUF

Twenty-one hours of talks in Islamabad produced nothing. Trump responded with immediate escalation: a full naval blockade of Iranian ports, effective 1000 ET today. The load-bearing assumption behind last week's market rally, that the ceasefire was a glide path to a deal, just cracked. Oil surged past $104, futures dropped across the board, and the two-week diplomatic window is now a coercion window. The Pentagon claims decisive victory in Operation Epic Fury: 13,000 targets struck, 80% of Iran's nuclear and missile infrastructure destroyed. But target counts are not political outcomes. Iran's strategy from the start has been to shift the war from firepower to endurance, and six weeks in, the Strait of Hormuz, open when the war began, remains effectively closed. Air superiority has not produced the political capitulation it was supposed to buy. On the far side of the table, China watches closely. Bonnie Glaser's assessment that Taiwan action remains unlikely near-term is significant, but Xi's PLA ideological rectification campaign suggests a leader tightening internal control, not one satisfied with his military's readiness.

Deep Reads
Foreign Affairs

Why the Cease-Fire With Iran Will Hold

By Gideon Rose. Argues the ceasefire holds because both sides were hurting and would hurt more from escalation. The de-escalatory logic that produced it is the same logic that sustains it: mutual exhaustion rather than mutual agreement.
Source may be paywalled
SWAP's Take

Rose's thesis aged poorly in the twelve hours between publication and the Hormuz blockade announcement. The argument that mutual exhaustion sustains ceasefires has historical support (Korea 1953, the Iran-Iraq War), but it depends on both sides reading the cost-benefit calculus the same way. The problem is asymmetric pain thresholds. America's pain is economic: gas prices, inflation, market volatility. Iran's pain is existential: 80% of its nuclear infrastructure, its navy, its supreme leader. When one side is annoyed and the other is fighting for survival, "mutual exhaustion" doesn't describe the same condition. The better parallel may be the 1991 Gulf War ceasefire: the U.S. stopped short of its maximum military objective and spent the next decade managing the consequences.

Foreign Affairs

How a Cease-Fire Can Lead to Disaster

By Chardell and Helfont. Draws 1991 Gulf War lessons: the greatest challenge is learning to live with a settlement that leaves the regime in place. Half-measures after military campaigns often create worse problems than the original conflict.
Source may be paywalled
SWAP's Take

This is the piece the administration should be reading but probably isn't. The 1991 parallel is analytically precise: overwhelming air campaign, impressive target counts, regime survives, and the post-war settlement creates a generation of consequences no one anticipated. The current administration has defined success as Iran without nukes, without a navy, without Hormuz leverage, and without proxy networks. Achieving all four through airpower alone has no historical precedent. Achieving some and living with the rest requires exactly the kind of strategic patience this administration has shown no appetite for. Cite them for the diagnosis. The prescription they don't offer: what does "living with the regime" actually require in terms of deterrent posture, regional architecture, and domestic political messaging?

What the Iran War Means for China

By Zongyuan Zoe Liu. The Iran war is the most consequential test of China's strategic restraint. Unlike Ukraine, this conflict threatens China's core interests: energy supply through Hormuz, Belt and Road investments, and the narrative that American military adventurism creates Chinese opportunities.
Source may be paywalled
SWAP's Take

Liu identifies the right question but underweights the answer. China's calculus isn't about restraint; it's about harvest. Every week the strait stays closed, China's overland energy infrastructure (Power of Siberia, CPEC, Central Asian routes) appreciates in strategic value. Beijing doesn't need to intervene to win from this war. The longer it runs, the more the global energy architecture shifts toward pipelines China already controls. The social feed today shows Glaser questioning whether China-Iran military coordination is happening without Xi's approval. The smarter question is whether Xi needs to coordinate militarily at all when the economic geography is doing the work for him. The real risk isn't Chinese intervention. It's Chinese patience.

Foreign Policy

The Iran War's Exit Ramp Has Become a Diplomatic Maze

The future status of the Strait of Hormuz is the central unresolved question. Iran views it as a toll booth. Trump floated a U.S.-Iran joint venture. Pakistan's framework was rejected. The exit ramp is now a maze of competing demands.
SWAP's Take

The maze metaphor is apt but understates the problem. A maze has a solution. The Hormuz question may not, because the sides aren't negotiating over the same object. For the U.S., the strait is a global commons. For Iran, it's sovereign-adjacent territory and the only leverage that survived the air campaign. You can't split the difference on a waterway. The "joint venture" idea is creative but operationally absurd: imagine the U.S. Navy and the IRGC co-managing vessel inspections. The indicator to watch: if Iran begins allowing non-U.S.-flagged commercial traffic while blocking American vessels, they're testing whether the world will accept a two-tier strait. That would set enormous precedent for other chokepoints worldwide.

War on the Rocks

How the War with Iran Ends

Examines three endgames: negotiated settlement, prolonged attrition, or regime collapse from within. Unlike Iraq 2003, Iran's population has repeatedly shown appetite for civil governance. The precision air campaign may give Iranians space to shape what comes next.
SWAP's Take

Within the scope of a practitioner piece, this is a well-constructed argument. The distinction between Iraq 2003 and Iran 2026 is analytically important and underappreciated. But the piece reaches beyond its evidence when it suggests precision strikes can surgically remove coercive capacity while preserving governance. The IRGC isn't a tumor you can excise. It's the circulatory system of the Iranian state, running everything from construction to internet infrastructure. Destroying its military capacity doesn't automatically free civil society; it can create a power vacuum that security forces fill with whatever remains. The optimistic scenario requires something the piece doesn't address: who fills the gap between the IRGC's collapse and civil governance's emergence? That gap is where post-war disasters live.

CSIS

The 2026 National Defense Strategy by the Numbers

CSIS breaks down the NDS: homeland defense, China deterrence, burden-sharing, industrial base. The strategy avoids labeling China a "strategic competitor" and omits Taiwan entirely (despite the NSS emphasizing it). Burden-sharing shifts from encouraged to required.
SWAP's Take

The NDS's refusal to call China a competitor while building a First Island Chain denial defense is strategic doublespeak that only makes sense if you're trying to deter without provoking. The Taiwan omission is the most interesting editorial choice: either deliberate ambiguity or honest reflection of reduced bandwidth during a Middle East war. CSIS correctly identifies that success depends on allies accepting burden-sharing, but the analysis doesn't connect this to the NATO withdrawal threat happening simultaneously. You can't demand 5% GDP defense spending from allies while threatening to leave the alliance that makes the spending meaningful. The NDS describes selective leadership. The administration's behavior describes reflexive threats. Those produce very different allied responses.

Lawfare

Understanding Iran's Strategy: Then, Now, and Next

Despite killing the supreme leader and senior officials, the government remains intact. Mojtaba Khamenei's selection demonstrates continuity. The U.S. and Israel "cannot just kill their way to a change in Iran's grand strategy."
SWAP's Take

Decapitation strikes produce succession, not capitulation. Mojtaba Khamenei's installation was the regime's answer to the air campaign: hereditary succession in a republic founded to reject hereditary rule, justified by existential threat. Pressure that's intense enough to threaten survival but not enough for regime change tends to consolidate the regime's hold. The population rallies around the flag, not the opposition. This is the lesson of every strategic bombing campaign from London to Hanoi to Baghdad: break the state completely or strengthen the government's narrative. There is very little middle ground, and the current campaign appears to be standing in exactly that middle ground.

The Strait remains effectively closed. Damaged fields will take years to resume. Oil up 80% YTD. Blackouts in Asia, fuel rationing in Europe. This is the biggest supply disruption ever. The piece argues the energy crisis has its own timeline independent of the war's resolution.
SWAP's Take

The critical insight is the time lag between peace and recovery. Even if a deal materialized tomorrow, damaged infrastructure doesn't repair on political timelines. The 1973 comparison understates the problem: that embargo was a political choice by producers who could restart pumping. This disruption includes physically destroyed facilities requiring years of reconstruction. The food crisis angle everyone else is underplaying: the strait carries fertilizer precursors. The UN's three-month warning on planting decisions means agricultural consequences are being locked in now, regardless of diplomacy. This is the cascading risk outside the war's narrative frame that may produce more human suffering than the bombing campaign itself.
