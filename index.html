Bottom Line Up Front
  • The US carried out its first strike on Iran since the peace memorandum, hitting missile, drone, and radar sites after Tehran attacked a cargo ship in the Strait of Hormuz. CENTCOM framed the strikes as a response to the attack on commercial shipping, and the vice president warned that "violence will be met with violence." Day 119 flagged the strait as the venue where the unresolved questions would get litigated with live fire; Day 120 is the litigation. A memorandum that ended the war on paper did not end the fight over who controls the chokepoint, and the moment Tehran tested it with a tanker, Washington answered with airpower. The settlement is not dead, but it is now being enforced by strike package rather than by signature.
  • The US brokered a Lebanon-Israel framework agreement in Washington, a deal whose real target is Tehran. After four days of talks, Israel and Lebanon signed a framework for a slight Israeli withdrawal and a path the administration is calling a first step toward lasting peace, with Iran pointedly cut out of the arrangement. The strategic logic is the same instinct behind the strikes: reduce Iran's regional leverage by settling the borders it uses its proxies to keep unstable. If the Hezbollah file can be moved into a diplomatic channel, one of Tehran's oldest sources of pressure loses value. Whether Hezbollah accepts a framework negotiated over its head is the question the signing ceremony cannot answer.
  • A catastrophic earthquake in Venezuela killed more than 900 people, and the US sent C-17s, satellite imagery, warships, and a Marine general into the relief effort. SOUTHCOM moved fast, with the rescue window closing as the toll climbed past 900 and international teams raced to find survivors. The deployment is humanitarian on its face and strategic underneath: a large, visible US presence in a country Washington has spent years treating as an adversary's client state. Disaster relief is the rare mission that buys influence without firing a shot, and the speed of the C-17 flow suggests someone in the building understood the opening.
  • The House advanced a $1.1 trillion Pentagon funding bill that also renames the department, while the force absorbed two reminders of how thin its margins are. Appropriators approved the topline alongside a "Department of War" redesignation, even as the F-35 began shipping without radars and analysts warned that reopening the Strait of Hormuz could take a yearslong multinational mine-clearing mission. The budget is enormous and still not elastic: the same week it crosses a trillion dollars, the marquee fighter ships incomplete and the Gulf cleanup gets priced in years. Size is not the same as readiness, and Day 120 keeps proving it.
  • John Bolton pleaded guilty to mishandling classified information, closing one of the highest-profile prosecutions of a former Trump official by the Trump administration. The former national security adviser admitted to retaining classified material; the president responded that he hoped Bolton "will be dealt with harshly." The case sits at the intersection of two recurring threads: the politicization of national-security enforcement and the spectacle of an administration prosecuting its own former principals. However one reads the merits, a guilty plea from a former NSA is a serious institutional event, and the president's public call for harsh treatment of a named defendant is its own kind of signal.
The Through-Line

Day 120 is American power working both ends of the same problem in the same week. In the Gulf, it is the blunt end: Iran fires on a ship, the US fires back at radar sites, and the peace memorandum gets enforced with ordnance. In Washington, it is the diplomatic end: a Lebanon-Israel framework signed under American auspices, designed to drain Iran's regional leverage without a shot. In the Caribbean, it is the soft end: C-17s and a Marine general into earthquake-stricken Venezuela, influence bought with relief. Three instruments, one strategy, all pointed at the same adversary's network of pressure points. The connective tissue is that none of it is free, and the Pentagon's own books say so. A $1.1 trillion bill still ships fighters without radars and prices the Hormuz cleanup in years. The strategy is coherent and the means are stretched, which is the durable condition of the moment: a country with the reach to strike, broker, and rescue all at once, and the accounting to remind it that doing all three leaves nothing in reserve.

How It Aged

Aged Well June 26 call: "In a negotiated settlement, watch the chokepoint, not the communique. The party that can still impose costs at the geographic pressure point holds leverage long after the formal fighting stops, and it will use it until the terms are set." Verdict: confirmed, faster than expected. Within twenty-four hours Iran attacked a cargo ship in Hormuz and the US struck Iranian missile, drone, and radar sites in response. The chokepoint was exactly where the settlement broke, and the toll fight escalated from evacuation pauses to airstrikes. Lesson: a memorandum that leaves the contested geography unresolved does not end the contest; it relocates it to the map. The strait was always going to be where this got decided, and the leverage there is measured in who is willing to shoot, not who signed.

Hormuz: The Memorandum Meets Live Fire
CENTCOMIran
US carries out its first strike on Iran since the peace memorandum was signed
The War Zone / Air & Space Forces Magazine · cross-corroborated
US forces struck Iranian missile, drone, and radar sites, the first American strikes on Iran since the peace memorandum, after Tehran attacked a commercial cargo ship in the Strait of Hormuz. The take: this is the memorandum's first real stress test, and it failed in the direction the geography predicted. The settlement assumed Iran would accept the postwar status quo at the chokepoint; instead Tehran tried to monetize it, and the US chose to answer a strike on shipping with a strike on military infrastructure rather than a diplomatic note. The choice matters. Hitting radar and missile sites signals that freedom of navigation, not the broader deal, is the red line Washington will enforce kinetically. The risk is that each side now has a demonstrated willingness to use force inside a framework both still claim to support, which is how a paused war becomes a simmering one.
USCENTCOMTargeting
US strikes Iranian missile, drone, and radar sites
Military Times / Air Force Times / Task & Purpose · cross-corroborated
CENTCOM said the strikes targeted the systems Iran would use to threaten shipping and US forces in the area: missile launchers, drone facilities, and the radars that cue them. The take: the target set is the tell. Going after radar and missile infrastructure rather than leadership or nuclear sites keeps the strike calibrated to the provocation, a response scaled to deter further attacks on shipping without reopening the maximalist war. It is escalation management by target list, and it reflects a force that learned from the first phase how expensive an unbounded campaign gets. The open question is whether calibrated strikes deter a regime that just demonstrated it will test the deal, or whether they simply set the price of the next test.
IranRetaliation
Iran fires drones at Bahrain in the first tit-for-tat strikes after the US hit Iran
South China Morning Post / ABC News / CBS News · cross-corroborated
Iran launched drones at Bahrain, which accused Tehran of a targeted strike on naval facilities, the first retaliation after the American strikes and the move that turns a one-sided response into an exchange. Bahrain hosts US Naval Forces Central Command and the Fifth Fleet. The take: the target choice is the escalation. By hitting Bahrain rather than US ships directly, Tehran answers the American strike while aiming at the softer, sovereign partner that houses the US naval presence, imposing a cost on Washington's posture without striking US forces head-on. It puts a Gulf monarchy in the line of fire and tests whether the US will treat an attack on a host nation's base as it would an attack on its own. This is the tit-for-tat cycle the memorandum was supposed to foreclose, now running in both directions inside a deal both sides still claim to honor.
HormuzMine Warfare
Reopening the Strait of Hormuz could require a yearslong multinational mission, analysts say
Stars and Stripes · single source, tracking
Analysts told Stars and Stripes that fully reopening and securing the Strait of Hormuz, including clearing any mines and re-establishing safe transit, could take a multinational effort measured in years rather than weeks. The take: this is the cleanup bill the BLUF keeps warning about, stated in time rather than dollars. Mine clearance is slow, dangerous, and coalition-dependent, and it ties up scarce mine-countermeasures assets that the Navy has underinvested in for a generation. The strcategic point is that a war can end in an afternoon and its aftermath can occupy the fleet for years. Whoever can threaten the strait cheaply forces everyone else into an expensive, open-ended commitment to keep it open, which is the asymmetry Iran has always understood better than its budget would suggest.

The US is flowing C-17 transports, satellite imagery, and other support to Venezuela after a devastating earthquake, with the War Zone tracking cargo jets moving into the Caribbean for the relief effort. The take: the airlift is the point and the politics are the subtext. The hard part is keeping it humanitarian: the same flights that save lives also map the country, and the imagery support makes that dual character explicit.

The relief mission is real; the demonstration effect is the dividend.
Force, Systems, and the Building
F-35Acquisition
It's official: F-35s are now being delivered without radars
The War Zone · single source
The War Zone reports that new F-35s are now being delivered without their radars installed, a consequence of supply and configuration issues that leaves freshly built jets short of a core combat system. The take: a fighter without a radar is a very expensive trainer, and shipping them this way is what a stressed industrial base looks like up close. The program is delivering airframes to keep the line moving and the delivery numbers up, then backfilling the mission systems later, which trades a clean headline for a hollow one. It rhymes with this week's broader budget story: the topline crosses a trillion dollars while the marquee platform ships incomplete. Quantity on the ramp is not the same as capability in the fight, and the gap between them is exactly where wartime readiness fails.

Soliciting ideas is cheap; building magazines of thousand-mile weapons at scale is the procurement problem of the decade.

Boxing Out Iran
LebanonIsrael
Israel and Lebanon sign a framework agreement in Washington
Axios / The Hill / BBC · cross-corroborated
After four days of US-brokered talks in Washington, Israel and Lebanon signed a framework agreement providing for a modest Israeli withdrawal and a path the administration is calling a first step toward lasting peace and security, with the secretary of state hailing it and Iran conspicuously excluded. The take: the diplomacy and the airstrikes are two faces of one strategy. Both aim to shrink Iran's regional leverage; the strikes do it by force at the chokepoint, the framework does it by moving the Hezbollah border problem into a channel Tehran does not control. If the deal sticks, it weakens one of Iran's most durable pressure tools. The hard part is Hezbollah itself, which was not at the table and has its own incentives. A framework signed over a proxy's head is a beginning, not a settlement, and the test is whether Beirut can deliver what Washington and Jerusalem just signed.

Just Security · single source, analysis
Just Security analyzes the Supreme Court's Mullin v. Doe decision, which it argues sanitizes and renders effectively unreviewable the termination of Temporary Protected Status for roughly 1.3 million people, exposing Haitian and Syrian beneficiaries to removal. The take: the consequential move is procedural, not substantive. The human stakes are immediate, the institutional stakes are larger, and both arrive the same week the country prepares to celebrate its 250th birthday, a juxtaposition that will define how the semiquincentennial reads.

Force and Strategy
War on the RocksDeterrence
Not imaginary: the deterrence gap is real and America needs low-yield nukes
War on the Rocks · analysis
War on the Rocks makes the case that a genuine gap exists at the low end of the nuclear ladder, arguing the US needs more flexible, lower-yield options to deter limited adversary use. The take: this is the perennial fight over whether usable nuclear options strengthen deterrence or lower the threshold for use, and the piece comes down firmly on the strengthen side. The strongest version of the argument is that an adversary who believes the US has only catastrophic options may gamble that it will not respond to a limited strike, and a credible low-yield rung closes that gap. The strongest counter, which the piece underweights, is that every new rung is also a new temptation, and that proliferating tailored options can normalize nuclear use rather than deter it. Where one lands depends on whether deterrence is read as a problem of credibility or of restraint.
