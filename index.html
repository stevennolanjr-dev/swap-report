BLUF — Bottom Line Up Front

The ceasefire-blockade paradox enters its fourth day with both sides publicly denying what the AP reports they privately agreed to: an in-principle extension of the truce past April 21. Pakistan's army chief landed in Tehran yesterday carrying a message from Washington, which means the channel is active even while the blockade squeezes. Markets are buying the optimism at face value, pushing the S&P past 7,000 for the first time and treating the war as a solved problem. They are pricing the political timeline, not the physical one. Even a signed deal won't reopen Hormuz overnight: mine-clearing alone takes weeks, damaged Iranian oil infrastructure takes years, and the fertilizer-precursor cascade is already in motion. Meanwhile, the IMF's April outlook quantifies what everyone can feel: growth slashed to 3.1% in the best case, 2% in the severe scenario, and Russia pocketing an estimated $45–151 billion windfall that directly funds its Sumy offensive. The war America is fighting is generating revenue for the war America wants to end.

War on the Rocks
“Choke Point: The Risks and Realities of America’s Iran Blockade”
Analysis of the operational, legal, and strategic dimensions of the U.S. naval blockade of Iranian ports. Examines the complexity of enforcing a blockade in a 21-mile strait bordered by hostile territory, the mine-clearing challenge, and the escalation risks inherent in interdicting third-party shipping.
SWAP’s Take

The blockade works as a pressure tool precisely because it doesn’t need to be perfect to be effective. Stopping 90% of Iranian trade while a sanctioned Chinese tanker slips through is still economic strangulation. The operational risk isn’t enforcement failure; it’s escalation from within: an Iranian mine, a drone swarm, or a third-party vessel that refuses to comply. The 1988 Operation Praying Mantis parallel is instructive: the last time the U.S. fought Iran in these waters, a one-day engagement destroyed half of Iran’s navy. Iran’s surviving asymmetric capability is the variable that keeps CENTCOM deploying 10,000 personnel for what is technically a peacetime operation during a ceasefire.

“Tactical Success, Strategic Failure? Washington Walks the Path to Defeat in Iran”
Argues that six weeks after launching Operation Epic Fury, the U.S. has achieved tactical objectives (destroyed nuclear facilities, killed the supreme leader) but lacks a coherent political end state. The ceasefire is already fracturing. The piece questions whether the administration defined success before it defined the campaign.
SWAP’s Take

The piece asks the Clausewitzian question that the administration hasn’t answered publicly: what political object does the military action serve? Killing a supreme leader and destroying nuclear facilities are means, not ends. If the end state is Iran without nukes, navy, Hormuz leverage, or proxy networks, airpower alone has never achieved all four simultaneously in any historical case. The closest parallel is Iraq 1991: tactical brilliance, strategic ambiguity, and a generation of consequences. Cite the diagnosis. Build your own prescription.

World Economic Outlook, April 2026: “Global Economy in the Shadow of War”
Three scenarios from 3.1% to 2% global growth depending on conflict duration. Inflation raised to 4.4%. MENA growth cratered from 3.9% to 1.1%. The severe scenario (2% growth) has been breached only four times since 1980. Risks described as decisively on the downside.

When the institution whose job is optimistic framing says risks are “decisively on the downside,” the downside is worse than they’re printing.

War on the Rocks
“Regime Change in Iran, Underpants Gnomes, and the Phase II Problem”
Argues that the administration’s end state implicitly requires regime change but no one will say so. The title references the South Park episode where gnomes steal underpants with a plan of Phase 1: Collect Underpants, Phase 2: ?, Phase 3: Profit. The piece argues U.S. Iran policy has the same gap: Phase 1 (destroy stuff), Phase 2 (?), Phase 3 (Iran complies).
SWAP’s Take

The Phase II problem is the sharpest framing of the war’s strategic gap. Destroying nuclear facilities is Phase I. A compliant Iran is Phase III. But Phase II requires either regime change (which the administration denies seeking), regime capitulation (which has no historical precedent under these conditions), or permanent military coercion (which the IMF just quantified as globally destabilizing). The piece is right that the gap between tactical achievement and political outcome is where wars go to become permanent commitments. Iraq, Afghanistan, and Libya all had brilliant Phase Is and catastrophic Phase IIs.

War on the Rocks
“Iran’s Other Front: The War Over the Internet”
Examines Iran’s information warfare campaign running parallel to the kinetic conflict. Iran has leveraged internet shutdowns, disinformation, and narrative control domestically while proxy media networks amplify anti-U.S. messaging regionally.
SWAP’s Take

The information front is where Iran retains capability even after losing 80% of its nuclear infrastructure and most of its navy. You can bomb a centrifuge but not a narrative. Iran’s domestic internet control serves dual purpose: suppressing internal dissent while controlling the war’s framing for an Iranian audience. The lesson from Ukraine’s information war success is that the side that controls its own narrative domestically has strategic resilience even when losing kinetically.
