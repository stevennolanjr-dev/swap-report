Deep Reads
Foreign Policy

What Happens if the U.S.-Iran Cease-Fire Collapses?

FP's four-scenario map for ceasefire collapse: limited tit-for-tat at sea, full Hormuz re-closure plus Houthi Bab el-Mandeb activation, direct strikes on Iranian energy infrastructure, and a Gulf-state-initiated off-ramp. Each scenario carries a different oil-price tail and alliance-cost tail.

Source may be paywalled — preview accessible.

SWAP's Take

The piece's real value is naming the Bab el-Mandeb as the second chokepoint — the Houthi option is what the oil market is underpricing, because the coalition that suppressed Houthi activity in 2024-25 is now distracted by Iran and short on munitions. The second chokepoint is cheaper to close and harder to reopen than the first. The scenario the piece doesn't model: what if the Houthis act independently, without explicit Iranian direction, and the U.S. lacks the interceptor inventory to respond at scale? That is the scenario where a "limited" ceasefire collapse cascades into a shipping insurance crisis within two weeks. The historical parallel isn't 1973 — it's the 1984-88 tanker war, where freeze-and-thaw over chokepoints outlasted the shooting phase by years.

CSIS (Analysis) Deep Source

Last Rounds? Status of Key U.S. Munitions at the Iran War Ceasefire

Cancian and Park's April 21 assessment of seven systems: Tomahawk, JASSM, PrSM, Patriot, THAAD, SM-3, SM-6. Their conclusion: combat-capable but entering a "window of increased vulnerability" in the Western Pacific that industrial surge will take years to close.

SWAP's Take

The piece's strength is specificity; its thin spot is the industrial base wall. "Surge Tomahawk production" and "be able to surge Tomahawk production" are separated by roughly 24 months of subcontractor pipeline, tooling, and workforce. The policy prescription assumes the capacity exists to surge — it doesn't, yet. The more consequential question the report raises without answering: what does Beijing do with a public U.S. admission that its Pacific bin is empty until 2027-28? The answer isn't "invade Taiwan." It's patience and low-cost coercion campaigns that are cheap to run and that the U.S. cannot match in volume. The "window of vulnerability" the report names is the exact window in which coercion below the conventional threshold is cheapest to exploit. This report should be read alongside, not instead of, the CFR piece on Chinese strategic patience.

Foreign Affairs

How China and Russia Can Exploit the Iran War

FA argues Moscow gets an oil-revenue windfall and Beijing gets a two-year window of measurably thinner U.S. Pacific credibility. Both benefit; neither is the same kind of beneficiary.

Source may be paywalled.

SWAP's Take

The argument is directionally right but proportionally misweighted. Russia's windfall buys time on a war it was already winning slowly; it doesn't change the trajectory. China's gain is structural: a two-year window validated by the Cancian report, in which every coercive move short of conventional war is cheaper than it was before Iran. The piece treats both as comparable beneficiaries — they're not. Russia got tactical relief. China got a shift in the correlation of forces. A patient adversary exploits an empty magazine more reliably than an impatient one. The piece would be improved by treating Chinese patience as a strategy rather than as a disposition.

War on the Rocks

How the War with Iran Ends

Three termination scenarios: negotiated settlement, prolonged attrition, and internal regime collapse. The piece's premise is that precision targeting of regime coercive tools while leaving civilian governance intact gives Iranians agency over what comes next.

SWAP's Take

The premise that regime and state can be cleanly separated is doing most of the work here — and history is not kind to it. Iraq 2003 was the last time U.S. planners made the same bet. The failure wasn't targeting accuracy; it was the structural assumption. The WotR piece is well-constructed practitioner analysis on the operational layer, but the civilian-cascade timeline from infrastructure damage runs on months, not days. Grid disruption, bridge damage, and refinery targeting have consequences that bleed into civilian governance on a timeline the piece's scenarios don't account for. Cite the author for the operational analysis. Build your own assessment of civilian-cascade timing separately.

RAND (Commentary) Deep Source

Israel's Lebanese Campaign Will Backfire

RAND argues the Israeli posture in southern Lebanon — capitalizing on Iranian weakness — is building a friction trap that empowers Hezbollah's resistance narrative just as the organization's external support is most vulnerable. Recommends rapid transition to a Lebanese state-led security framework.

SWAP's Take

The piece lands the point most Israeli analysts haven't named: the moment to press an advantage and the moment to consolidate it are different moments, and conflating them costs more than the original advantage. The 1982 parallel is the one the authors reach for without naming: prolonged Israeli presence in south Lebanon after a decisive tactical phase created Hezbollah as a serious actor. This piece's open question is whether the current Israeli government has the domestic political room to execute a handoff — and the answer is probably no, which makes the RAND recommendation technically correct and politically undeliverable. That's worth knowing.

Foreign Policy

Pakistan Gambled on Mediation

FP's assessment of the Islamabad Track: Pakistan spent diplomatic capital to position the channel. If it works, it validates a non-Western mediation model. Now that Round 2 is dead, Islamabad absorbs the cost from both sides.

Source may be paywalled.

SWAP's Take

Today's BUSTED prediction on the peace proposal makes this piece more relevant, not less. The piece's core insight — mediators absorb blame when talks fail — just happened in real time. The underpriced variable remains China: Beijing was watching this channel for evidence that a non-Western mediation model could be exported. The Pakistani failure doesn't kill the model; it teaches Beijing to condition its own future mediation plays more carefully before stepping in publicly. Saudi Arabia-Iran 2023 succeeded because both parties wanted the deal. The Islamabad Track failed because Iran decided the blockade was not compatible with negotiation. China will internalize that lesson.

CFR (Analysis)

How the Iran War Confirmed, Contradicted, and Complicated U.S. Policy

CFR's retrospective on which standing U.S. strategic assumptions survived the Iran campaign. Survived: precision-strike dominance. Contradicted: industrial-base readiness. Complicated: alliance credibility in both the Gulf and Asia.

SWAP's Take

The "confirmed" column is the piece's weakest section. Precision strike was confirmed against a degraded adversary in a permissive air environment — not the Pacific. That nuance gets buried in a tone of "the toolkit still works." The better framing: Iran was the last conflict where the existing toolkit could be stretched across the mission. The next one won't allow it. The piece's real contribution is placing alliance complication as a first-order effect rather than a downstream consequence — that's the right call, and it's the framing that should inform the Spain NATO announcement and the Japan linkage story running in parallel this week.
