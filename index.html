HUDSONYouTube panel · May 7, 2026
Naval Warfare in the Second Nuclear Age

Bryan Clark and a panel of retired admirals analyze recent friction in Hormuz through the lens of "limited nuclear escalation." The panel argues that the U.S. Navy's surface posture is inadequately hardened against non-kinetic "dazzling" and high-power microwave attacks used as de-escalation signals. Key recommendation: distribute vertical launch system (VLS) cells across smaller, unmanned platforms to avoid "eggs in one basket" targeting.

SWAP's Take

The Clark argument is structurally consistent with the magazine-depth diagnosis. Concentrated VLS in a small number of capital ships becomes a magazine-depth target in regions where the adversary has cheap massed missiles and is willing to use them. The distributed-VLS prescription assumes the U.S. industrial base can field unmanned surface platforms at the rates required, which is the bottleneck the Plumb conversation also names. Two pieces today, same diagnosis from different angles, same prescription path. Expect this Hudson piece to be cited in the next round of Force Design reviews.

RANDResearch report · May 7, 2026
How to Forecast China's Lithography Leap: A Comparative Analysis (RAND)

Delphi method forecasting on China's path to indigenous 7nm lithography without Western tools. Crowdsourced expert forecasting is significantly more pessimistic about China's timeline than official DoD assessments, citing systemic friction in chemical supply chains that hardware-centric analysts miss. Recommends a shift in export controls from "machines" to "precursor chemicals."

SWAP's Take

The methodological move is the contribution. Delphi-method forecasting outperforms DoD assessment because the experts pulled in are closer to the chemistry than the machines, and the bottleneck is in the chemistry. The policy implication is uncomfortable for the export-control architecture: precursor chemicals are harder to track, harder to interdict, and dual-use in ways the existing regime is not built around. The piece's prescription is structurally right and operationally hard. Expect Commerce Bureau of Industry and Security to push back on the implementation difficulty rather than the analytical conclusion.
