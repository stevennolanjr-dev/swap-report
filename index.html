How It Aged
Busted Islamabad Talks: Mutual Restraint Language by Monday

The April 25 claim was that the Islamabad proximity-talks format would produce a Pakistani joint statement by April 27 using “mutual steps” or “sequenced measures” language, signaling preliminary framework convergence. It did not. The talks ended without an MoU, without a joint statement of substance, and with both sides describing the outcome as “stalled.” Trump publicly characterized Iran as “unyielding” on the nuclear file. The structural pattern that fits Reykjavik, Oslo, and the 2013 Geneva backchannel did not hold here, the Islamabad track was not converging on a deal that was being negotiated for announcement; it was negotiating substance and failing to bridge it. Lesson: venue convergence is necessary but not sufficient. Pakistan was the right venue for both sides; the gap on the nuclear file was bigger than the choreography could close. The framework that surfaced via Axios 24 hours later is the actual signal of where the real conversation is happening, and it sits outside the Pakistani channel entirely.

Partial Touska Retaliation: Doctrine or Improvisation

The April 21 claim was that Iranian retaliation for the Touska seizure would extend through proxy-executed action against soft allied targets, with the diagnostic question being whether a second Western capital was hit within seven days, a doctrinal pattern, or whether London remained alone, opportunistic. The London arson campaign continued through the week; Harakat Ashab al-Yamin, an IRGC-aligned proxy group, claimed multiple synagogue attacks. No second Western capital was struck inside the seven-day window. The doctrinal-versus-opportunistic question therefore resolves to opportunistic-but-sustained: the IRGC has demonstrated the proxy channel works against London soft targets, but the absence of a Paris, Berlin, or Brussels companion incident suggests the playbook is being run inside one operating envelope rather than as a coordinated multi-city campaign. The retaliation is real; the geographic ambition implied by the original claim was wider than the actual operation has been. Lesson: distinguish between a proxy channel being exercised, which the London pattern confirms, and a multi-city doctrinal campaign, which the absence of a second capital weakens. The next 30 days will tell which way the pattern drifts.

Partial UK Attribution Speed: Iran or Generic “Hostile State”

The April 21 claim was that UK attribution on the London arson attacks would publicly name Iran (directly or through named proxy groups) within seven days, with a slow or generic attribution signaling London’s preference to protect a diplomatic off-ramp. The Met Police and Home Office have publicly confirmed they are “investigating Iranian proxies,” including the Harakat Ashab al-Yamin claim, but have not formally attributed the attacks to the Islamic Republic. The framing is closer to the “hostile state” cautious tier than to a clean Iran call. Three charges have been filed for the Iran International TV arson; the broader synagogue pattern remains under investigation. Lesson: London is preserving optionality. The decision to investigate proxies publicly while declining to name Iran formally is itself a political signal, the UK is positioning to escalate attribution if a second incident forces it and to walk it back if the diplomatic track produces movement. That is hedging, not weakness; it should be read as such.

Foreign Policy · Analysis
Iran Is More Unified Than Ever
Foreign Policy · April 28, 2026
The piece argues that the Iran war has produced not regime collapse, the original Western theory of victory, but the opposite: a domestic consolidation around Khamenei and Qalibaf that has visibly hardened Iranian negotiating posture. The analysis traces the rallying-around-the-flag dynamic, the IRGC’s relative gain in domestic standing, and the way the war has eclipsed the pre-war legitimacy critique that had been gathering momentum.
SWAP’s Take

This piece is the analytical mirror of the framework reported this morning. If Tehran is more unified than it was at the start of the war, then the framework Iran put on the table is the deal Tehran can sign at home, and any U.S. counter-proposal that demands more concession will get less, not more, traction. The piece is also a corrective to the “regime collapse” thesis that has been the implicit theory of victory for parts of the Washington policy community since February. Wars produce all kinds of internal political effects; the assumption that maximum pressure produces collapse is a specific historical claim that has held in some cases (interwar Italy) and not in others (Saddam-era Iraq, Castro-era Cuba, post-2014 Russia). The relevant historical pattern here is closer to the latter cluster than to the former. The Mohammed regime-collapse thesis SWAP analyzed on April 15 has not been borne out by the war’s first 60 days; this piece is the first major published essay openly making that point.

Foreign Policy · Politics
The Iran War Is Tearing Trump’s Coalition Apart
Foreign Policy · April 28, 2026
A close read of the fissures inside the second-term Trump coalition that the Iran war has opened. SWAP’s Take

The coalition fissure analysis is correctly framed as a structural problem, not a Trump-management problem. Restraint MAGA wants the war ended; America-First wants it ended without nuclear concession; China-hawks want resources back to the Pacific; Israel-hawks want the nuclear program eliminated. The Iranian framework reported today exposes the gap between those four positions. Accepting the framework satisfies restraint MAGA and China-hawks; rejecting it satisfies America-First and Israel-hawks. There is no version of the deal that satisfies all four, which is why the administration’s public stance, dismiss the proposal as a non-starter, is structurally easier to maintain than any concrete counter-proposal would be. The political logic of rejection is stronger than the strategic logic of acceptance, until the cost of continued operations forces the calculus to flip. Watch the FY27 budget testimony this week; that is where the cost of continued operations becomes a Senate floor question.

War on the Rocks · China
The Mountaintop Mirage: Why Xi’s Military Purges Cannot Produce the Force He Wants
War on the Rocks · April 28, 2026
The author argues that Xi’s ongoing PLA purges are producing institutional fear, not institutional capability. The piece traces the historical pattern, Stalin’s Red Army, Saddam’s officer corps, post-coup Turkish armed forces, and argues that loyalty-tested militaries underperform combat-tested ones in initial weeks of a major contingency. The argument has direct implications for Western planning assumptions about a Taiwan timeline.
SWAP’s Take

Within the scope of a War on the Rocks essay, this is a tightly constructed argument and the historical pattern is well chosen. The piece is strongest in its diagnosis: loyalty purges produce a force that is politically reliable and operationally fragile, and Xi’s pattern is consistent with the loyalty model. Where the piece thins is in the prescription space, what does the U.S. do with the diagnosis? If the PLA is more politically reliable than operationally capable, then Western deterrence calculus has more time than the worst-case Taiwan timelines suggest, but only if the U.S. force structure can take advantage of that time. The piece doesn’t engage with that second-half question, which is the harder one. Cite the author for the diagnosis; build the planning prescription separately. The Bonnie Glaser claim from April 13, that PLA readiness anxiety, not Iran distraction, is the underweighted variable, fits the same pattern this piece identifies.
