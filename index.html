Through-line

Three ceasefires are fraying simultaneously and on the same calendar. The Iran ceasefire is on paper while the kinetics continue at calibrated levels: seven fast boats sunk, ten attacks on U.S. forces, an ADNOC tanker hit. The Israel-Lebanon ceasefire broke this week with the first Beirut strike since April 17 and roughly 110 dead in Lebanon since May 1. The Russia-Ukraine "Victory Day truce" registered 1,820 violations by 10 a.m. on May 6. Each pause has the same structure: declared, partial, fragile, with persistent below-threshold action and a public-private gap between official messaging and battlefield fact. The variable common to all three is whether the pause is the off-ramp or the reload. Markets are pricing the off-ramp; the analysts working the inventory math are pricing the reload. One of those reads is wrong, and the gap is widening.

Deep Reads
RANDPolicy Brief · May 6, 2026
Build Partner Airpower, Not Air Forces: Lessons Learned and Policy Recommendations

The brief argues U.S. security force assistance should shift from building traditional partner air forces to building partner "airpower" capabilities (UAS, ISR, precision effects). The collapse of the Afghan and Iraqi air forces is the load-bearing case study: sustainment of high-end airframes is a strategic liability in irregular warfare, and the U.S. industrial-base footprint required to keep partner-flown F-16s in the air is disproportionate to the operational return.

SWAP's Take

The piece earns its diagnosis. The Afghan-Iraqi parallel is the right one, and the prescription is appropriately bounded: swap "build partner air forces" for "build partner airpower capabilities the partner can actually sustain." The thin spot is the Indo-Pacific application, which the piece does not press hard enough on. Tier-two Indo-Pacific partners (PH, Vietnam, Indonesia) have more sustainment infrastructure than 2003 Iraq did, but the U.S. SFA architecture defaults to the Iraq-Afghanistan playbook because it is the institutional-memory pattern. Pair this with the WOTR magazine-depth piece for the homeland-industrial-base side; the partner-airpower argument and the magazine-depth argument both point at the same structural fix, which is industrial-base capacity rather than acquisition policy.

MILREVArmy University Press · May/June 2026 issue
Deep Battle and Deep Operations in Contemporary Warfare: the Transparent Battlefield

Capt. Randy Noorman analyzes how persistent ISR plus instant fires has forced low troop density and a return to "frontalism" in current combat. Suppressive assets, Noorman argues, have failed to keep pace with defensive transparency: armor and infantry under continuous observation cannot maneuver the way Cold War-era doctrine assumed. The piece prescribes a rethink of how offensive suppression operates when the defender sees everything in real time.

SWAP's Take

This is the cleanest "so what" piece on Ukraine and on the Iran-war ground-combat shape that has been written this spring. Noorman names the doctrinal gap with operational specificity: suppressive fires were calibrated for an opponent who could not see their own targeting solution unfold in real time, and the assumption no longer holds. The implication for U.S. force design is not abstract; it lands directly on the Force Design 2030 reconnaissance-forward push the Marines are running and on the Army's MDO architecture. The piece also quietly contradicts the drone-dominance hype cycle: drones are part of transparency, but the binding fix is offensive suppression at industrial scale, which is what the WOTR cheap-massed-missiles piece argues from the other direction. The two pieces should be read together. Expect doctrinal writers at MWI and War on the Rocks to cite Noorman within thirty days.

Acquisition reform needs its own wargame, run with the same seriousness as a campaign wargame.

SWAP's Take

The piece is methodologically right and politically inconvenient. Acquisition wargaming is harder than campaign wargaming because the counterfactuals stretch over ten- to fifteen-year horizons and the political-economy variables (industrial base, supplier consolidation, rare-earth dependency) are not native to the campaign-wargame community. The piece nonetheless prescribes the right next step. The institutional home for the work is probably CAPE or RAND PAF rather than the war colleges, and that is itself the political problem: acquisition reform cannot be wargamed in the same room as the appropriations decision it is supposed to inform. Expect CSIS to take the methodological lead inside ninety days.
