Through-line

Three of today's lead stories share one underlying claim: that asking how the war ends is itself disloyal. Hegseth said it explicitly to senators. The administration is saying it implicitly by letting the WPR clock run out without a vote. And the market is saying it in its own way by pricing oil down on a "ceasefire" that has neither lifted the U.S. blockade nor reopened Iranian ports. Each separately may be defensible. Together they describe a country whose institutional checks are being asked to wait quietly while a war finds its own ending.

Deep Reads
Hudson Institute · Bryan Clark
How Iran exposed the limits of the U.S. Navy
Clark argues the Iran war did not require Iran to defeat the U.S. Navy; it only required Iran to be a problem the Navy could not solve at acceptable cost. He pulls the carrier-strike-group math, the available-fleet ratios, and the maintenance-cycle realities together to show that a force optimized for distributed presence cannot also generate the high-tempo, sustained CENTCOM concentration the political demand requires.
SWAP’s Take

This is the most useful Hudson piece in months because Clark refuses the comfortable framing. The U.S. Navy is not "broken"; it is sized and structured for a different war. Iran did not win on the water; it won by making the water expensive enough that the political question of why we are there cannot be deferred forever. The historical analog is not Pearl Harbor and not 1990. It is the British 1956 Suez moment, when a force that was still globally dominant discovered, in one campaign, that dominance had a price its political economy would not pay. The American answer at the time was to absorb the lesson. The Hudson piece is asking whether Washington today can do the same.

Foreign Policy
Trump’s Plan B for tariffs rests on shaky foundations
A clean walk-through of the post-SCOTUS tariff workarounds: Section 232 national-security duties, Section 301 unfair-trade investigations, and a USTR theory that "national security tariffs" survive the IEEPA ruling because they were never IEEPA tariffs. The piece argues each workaround is weaker law than the regime SCOTUS just struck down, and that the political economy of refunding the $200B already collected is the unspoken constraint shaping every move.
Source: Foreign Policy. Free article window.
SWAP’s Take

Within the scope of an FP analytical column, this is well-constructed. It thins where the prescription should sit: the piece names the legal weakness of the workarounds without naming the practical consequence, which is that any importer with a competent trade lawyer now has an option to litigate any post-IEEPA tariff and collect on it. The administration’s actual bet is not legal; it is temporal, that political control of CBP enforcement matters more than appellate rulings two years out. That bet has worked before in this administration. It also has a short half-life. The reader should track the first major Section 232 challenge filed under the Learning Resources reasoning; if a circuit court extends the holding, the Plan B regime collapses faster than the Plan A regime did.

Foreign Affairs
China and America are courting nuclear catastrophe
The essay’s argument is that the absence of a working U.S.-China crisis-management channel during a hot Middle East war creates a structural risk that no current arms-control architecture is sized to handle. The authors marshal the Cuban Missile Crisis hotline analog, the 1995 Norwegian-rocket near-miss, and a forensic reconstruction of how thin the existing PLA-USINDOPACOM deconfliction wires actually are.
Source: Foreign Affairs. Paywall.
SWAP’s Take

The strongest part of this essay is the historical excavation; the weakest is the prescription, which collapses into an appeal for "more dialogue" without engaging why dialogue collapsed. The harder argument is the one the essay nearly makes and then pulls back from: deterrence theory built for a bipolar world has an unsolved gap when a third nuclear actor (Russia) is reduced to a spoiler role and a fourth (Iran) is rapidly racing toward a threshold capability under wartime cover. The authors are right that the off-ramps are not built. They underweight that nobody currently has a coherent theory of who should build them or what they should look like. Cite this for the diagnosis; build your own prescription.

War on the Rocks
Economic fury and claims of victory
A practitioner argument that the administration’s Iran-war messaging has migrated from "we are winning" to "we have won" without ever defining "won." The piece tracks the pivot from operational metrics (target counts) to economic metrics (Iranian inflation, sanctions compression) and warns that the latter is harder to falsify because the data is harder to verify and the timelines are longer than the news cycle.
SWAP’s Take

The diagnostic is sound: redefining victory mid-war is the oldest available political move. The piece thins as it moves from diagnosis to prescription, never quite naming the question the next administration will face: what does "win" mean when the regime did not fall, the nuclear program is closer to threshold than it was in February, and the Strait is open only on Iran’s sufferance. The author’s own framework suggests an extension the article does not take: the administration has not earned its claim of victory because it has not specified the political end-state the campaign was meant to produce. Use this piece to discipline your reading of every "victory" press release for the next 60 days.

Atlantic Council · Inflection Points
Mythos, not the Iran war, is the most significant geopolitical warning of our time
A Frederick Kempe column arguing that "Mythos," the Beijing-Moscow-Tehran-Pyongyang convergence narrative, is the more consequential strategic story than any single Iran-war battle line. The piece pulls together the 2026 China-Russia Arctic exercise, the new Iran-North Korea missile-tech swap reporting, and the strategic logic of a coordinated revisionist axis confronting a U.S. distracted in CENTCOM.
SWAP’s Take

Kempe’s instinct is right; his framing inflates. "Axis" is doing too much work. China-Russia tactical alignment is real and operational; China-Iran is transactional, not allied; North Korea remains the wild card. What the piece captures correctly is the shape of the strategic problem: the U.S. cannot generate force concentrations in CENTCOM without paying opportunity costs in the Indo-Pacific that adversaries are willing to compound. What it misses is that the same logic constrains Beijing, which still depends on the Strait of Malacca for energy. The right read is a competition of mutual constraints, not a coordinated revisionist march. That distinction matters because it changes the prescription.

Lawfare · Scott R. Anderson
The legal framework for accession
A primer on the statutory and treaty architecture governing how a state would actually accept being absorbed into another. The piece is occasioned by the Trump administration’s "Greenland conversation" and the looser "57th state" rhetoric circulating in some Trump-aligned circles. It walks through the U.S. constitutional process (a treaty ratified by two-thirds of the Senate plus implementing legislation), the international-law side (recognition, succession of obligations), and the historical analogs (Hawaii, Texas, Puerto Rico’s still-unresolved status).
SWAP’s Take

Anderson is doing the unglamorous public service of taking a half-serious political claim and walking it through the law. The result is useful in two directions. First, it documents how high the bar actually is, which is the news that the rhetoric obscures. Second, it surfaces the harder, less obvious question: even if a Greenland accession were politically thinkable, the U.S. has no playbook for absorbing a sovereign democratic polity that has, by domestic referendum, opted in. Hawaii and Texas are different cases. The legal architecture exists; the political architecture does not. That gap is where the Trump-era foreign-policy improvisation will keep finding work.
