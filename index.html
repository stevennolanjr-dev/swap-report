Bottom Line Up Front

The Iran framework is dead in public; the war is now in its second month with no off-ramp on the table that either side is willing to sign. Trump’s morning post warning Iran to “better get smart soon” landed as Brent crude pushed past $115 a barrel, the eighth straight session of gains, with WTI above $103 and the national average at the pump at $4.23. The Strait remains effectively closed to Iranian-flagged tonnage; the rest of the world’s tanker traffic is moving on insurance premiums that have quintupled. Markets are no longer pricing a near-term resolution. They are pricing the war as a structural condition.

Hegseth is in the hot seat, and Politico led with it this morning. The pressure is no longer about a single firing or a single leak; it is the cumulative weight of a SECDEF whose Senate room for the FY27 markup is shrinking faster than the calendar allows. Comey was indicted again Monday night, the second indictment after the first was tossed; the legal substance is thin and the political signal is loud. The FISA Section 702 reauthorization deadline hits Thursday with House Republicans publicly fighting each other rather than the Democrats. King Charles wraps the state visit today after an address that praised the alliance without quite naming the war.

Three structural items underneath. The Supreme Court heard arguments today on Trump’s removal of Temporary Protected Status for Haitians and Syrians; the case is the test of how much immigration discretion survives the second-term rewrite. The Navy awarded Ingalls $283 million to begin work on the FF(X) frigate, and Acting SecNav Hung Cao gave his vision speech: more ships, “Alpha” troops, and a generational pitch about not sending his son to the war he fought. The Space Force quietly proposed canceling the Polar Missile Warning program in the FY27 build, the first program kill of the cycle and a signal that the Golden Dome architecture is going to absorb everything around it.

SWAP’s Take

The Hegseth story is now a friction story, not a personality story. The SECDEF’s political will is intact. His operational reach inside Washington is shrinking. That is a different problem than scandal: scandal is binary, friction is cumulative. The historical parallel is not Rumsfeld in 2006, when one set of decisions had failed visibly. The mechanism then was Senate appropriators losing patience with answers they considered evasive; the mechanism now looks similar. The visible test is whether SASC marks up FY27 before Memorial Day. That is the inflection point to watch.

Polar Missile Warning is the first to go; FY27 markup will surface the next several. SWAP’s Take

This is now a strategic-bombing-meets-economic-warfare problem with two sets of bills coming due simultaneously. The U.S. theory of victory rests on the assumption that economic and military pressure will produce regime concession before American political will erodes. Six weeks in, nothing in the operational picture supports that assumption: the regime is consolidating, not fragmenting, and the war has, paradoxically, given Tehran the domestic legitimacy it had been losing. The Foreign Affairs piece this morning, “How the War Saved the Iranian Regime,” is making exactly that argument, and it is harder to dismiss the longer the war runs without a political result. The historical pattern that fits this is not Desert Storm. It is the bombing of North Vietnam, where target counts kept growing, the political object stayed elusive, and the bills, energy prices, alliance friction, domestic dissent, came due everywhere except the air campaign. The question for the President this week is not whether to escalate. It is whether the theory of victory has been earned by anything that has happened in the last sixty days. The honest answer is no.

Long-form analysis · Source may be paywalled
The argument: regime fragility, the dominant Western expectation through the protests of 2022 and the economic crisis of 2024-2025, has been reversed by the war. The bombing campaign, the naval blockade, and the visible Western commitment to regime damage have produced a domestic legitimacy effect Tehran could not have purchased. Internal opposition is muted; the IRGC is consolidated; the political class has closed ranks around survival rather than reform. The piece marshals interview evidence, opposition-figure statements, and economic data showing both deterioration in conditions and rallying domestic support for the regime that bears the deterioration.
SWAP’s Take

This argument is the one Western strategy has been failing to engage with for two months. Bombing campaigns rally populations against outsiders; this is one of the most consistent findings in the strategic-bombing literature. The Battle of Britain steeled British society. The 1972 Linebacker campaigns did not break Hanoi politically. The 1999 Kosovo campaign produced regime change only when paired with a credible ground threat that has no analog in the Iran case. The piece is well-marshaled within the scope of a Foreign Affairs essay; where it thins is on the prescription side, the diagnosis that the war has saved the regime does not yet generate a clear answer to the question of what U.S. policy should now do, given that the bombing campaign cannot be unwound politically. Cite it for the diagnosis; expect the prescription to be the next round of analytical work.

Foreign Policy
The Taiwan Evacuation Trap
Long-form analysis · Source may be paywalled
The argument: U.S. policy on civilian evacuation in a Taiwan crisis has not caught up to the operational realities of the modern PLA. The piece walks through the geometry of an evacuation under contested air and sea conditions, the lift requirements, the strategic-airlift constraints, and the political signaling problem of a visible NEO either too early (escalation) or too late (mass casualty). The author argues current Pentagon planning frames the problem as a deterrence question when it is actually an early-warning and pre-decisional execution question.
SWAP’s Take

The piece is doing the right work. The civilian evacuation problem in a Taiwan contingency is the part of the planning that gets the least public oxygen and the most quiet anxiety inside INDOPACOM. The author is honest about the asymmetry: NEO planning that looks credible deters; NEO execution that looks panicked accelerates. That is the trap. The historical comparison the author should have pushed harder on is Saigon 1975, which is the only precedent for a fully-contested NEO from a partner population larger than the lift available. The lessons of Frequent Wind, including ones the U.S. has internalized poorly, would have strengthened the prescription. Within the scope of a Foreign Policy essay, this is a serious piece. The reader should leave with the question of whether Pentagon planning treats Taiwan NEO as a Phase 0 problem or a Phase 2 problem; the piece argues it should be both, and it is right.

The Transatlantic MAGA Fantasy
Long-form analysis · Source may be paywalled
The argument: the European populist right's expectation that a second Trump term would deliver a transatlantic alignment with U.S. politics has not survived contact with the second-term's actual foreign policy. The piece traces the divergence: NATO friction is real and rising, the trade agenda is more confrontational with European partners than with strategic adversaries on some lines, and the cultural-political affinity that European populists projected onto Washington has not produced policy concessions. The author argues European political movements that built strategy around alignment with the second term should be rebuilding it.
SWAP’s Take

The diagnosis is correct and not new. The framing problem the piece does not quite resolve is that the European populist right was never strategically coherent on the transatlantic question; the alignment with Washington was an emotional projection, not a policy position. The Foreign Affairs essay treats it as a strategy that failed; it was closer to a vibe that didn’t survive the test. The historical analogue is the European left’s expectations of the Obama administration, also emotionally projected, also disappointed when policy made contact with national interest. The valuable contribution here is the reminder that alignment is built through interest convergence, not affinity, and that the European far right has misread that for nine months. The piece would be stronger if it pushed on what European policy should now do; it diagnoses without prescribing, which is fair within the scope of an opinion essay but leaves the reader without a takeaway.

War on the Rocks
Aligning the U.S. and Canadian Defense Industrial Bases
Practitioner essay
The argument: U.S. and Canadian defense industrial bases have grown apart in ways that constrain the binational continental defense mission, and the policy fixes are smaller than the political ones. The piece walks through procurement-rule misalignment, security-clearance friction, and the loss of integrated supply chains that NORAD planning depended on. The author is concrete on the fixes: harmonized ITAR exemptions, pre-cleared joint programs, binational fund structures.
SWAP’s Take

This is exactly the kind of essay that does not generate headlines and matters disproportionately. The continental defense mission is a politically uncontroversial place to build muscle for binational industrial integration; if the U.S. and Canada cannot align procurement on something with bipartisan defense support on both sides of the border, the larger industrial-base challenges with the UK, Australia, and Japan get harder. The author’s prescription is operationally clean. The political weight is the open question; the current bilateral relationship has more friction than this essay assumes. Cite it for the diagnosis; the prescription is correct in form and underweight on what would have to change politically before the mechanics could be implemented.

The Bulwark
We Still Haven’t Answered the Basic Questions of the Iran War
Opinion essay
The argument: sixty days into the war, the basic questions, what is the political object, what is the theory of victory, what are the conditions under which the U.S. would accept a negotiated end, are still publicly unanswered. The piece walks through how each administration speech, briefing, and press conference has avoided the questions rather than answered them. The author argues the absence of public answers is not coyness; it reflects an absence of internal consensus.
SWAP’s Take

The essay’s strongest claim is that the absence of articulated political object is not deception; it is the symptom of a process inside the administration that has not converged. That is consistent with the operational record: target counts grow, military pressure expands, and yet no White House readout has produced the sentence that says “the war ends when X.” In strategic-theory terms, this is the absence of the link between political purpose and military aim that determines whether bombing campaigns succeed politically or culminate without producing a political result. The piece is right to insist on the question. The reader is left with the right kind of discomfort, the gap is not because the administration won’t share its theory; it is because the theory does not appear to have been written.

Foreign Policy
Why Indonesia Floated a Malacca Toll
Analysis · Source may be paywalled
The argument: Jakarta's Malacca-toll trial balloon is not a revenue idea; it is a strategic positioning move under the cover of a fiscal proposal. With Hormuz contested and the world short on tanker geometry, Malacca is now the single-point-of-failure for Asian trade and energy flows that has not yet been priced. Indonesia raising the question of who pays for the externalities is, the author argues, a way to begin negotiating with China and the U.S. on the strait's security architecture without having to name it that way.
SWAP’s Take

The piece is sharp where similar Malacca essays usually go soft. Most analysis treats the Malacca question as a pure China-U.S. competition over a chokepoint; the author correctly reframes it as Indonesia using a fiscal lever to enter a security conversation it has been excluded from. That is a useful reframe. The historical analogue is Egypt’s Suez Canal Authority, which has spent fifty years using toll structures as the lever for a security and political conversation no one wants to name as such. Indonesia is reaching for the same playbook. Whether it works depends on Beijing and Washington both treating the toll as the front for the security conversation, which they may not. The signal to watch is whether the toll proposal is followed by a Jakarta-led ASEAN initiative on Malacca security architecture inside ninety days.

MWI at West Point
Missiles, Guns, Lasers, and Nets: The Case for Passive Drone Defenses
Practitioner essay
The argument: the active counter-UAS toolkit, missiles, guns, lasers, has consumed the budget conversation while passive defenses, decoys, nets, electromagnetic concealment, and physical hardening, do disproportionate work at a fraction of the cost. The piece marshals Ukraine and Iran-war operational data showing that passive defenses absorbed a meaningful fraction of strikes that would otherwise have required active interceptors. The author argues the kinetic-only orientation of the U.S. counter-UAS investment is misaligned with what is actually working in the field.
SWAP’s Take

This is a practitioner’s piece written with the discipline of someone who has watched programs of record consume budget without changing the operational picture. The Ukraine evidence base is real; the Iran-war data is real; the prescription, that passive defenses deserve a real share of the counter-UAS budget rather than a footnote, is correct. The structural problem the essay surfaces but does not solve is acquisition culture: nets and decoys do not produce contractor lines, generate program offices, or sustain test calendars. They are competitively boring. That is not a reason to underweight them; it is the reason they have been underweighted. Cite it; pair it with the Ukraine open-source assessment work that has been building the same case for three years.

The Bulwark
How Péter Magyar Toppled Viktor Orbán’s Illiberal Regime
Long-form analysis
The argument: Péter Magyar's electoral defeat of Orbán was not a black swan; it was the cumulative effect of three years of systematic political organizing inside an illiberal framework, paired with a corruption scandal that broke through the captured media environment. The piece walks through the organizational, financial, and messaging discipline that Magyar's Tisza Party brought to a system designed to make exactly that defeat impossible. Implications: illiberal entrenchment is more brittle than its propaganda suggests, and the political tools that defeat it are recognizable.
SWAP’s Take

This is the companion piece to the Hungary special edition we published earlier this month. The Bulwark essay is doing the close-grain political-organization analysis the special edition could only summarize. The argument’s strongest contribution is to push back against the determinist read of illiberal regimes, that once captured, electoral systems cannot recover, with concrete organizational evidence to the contrary. The lesson generalizes: where institutional capture is not yet complete, organizational discipline plus a precipitating scandal is sufficient. Where capture is more advanced, neither is. Hungary is an instructive case because the capture was deep but not total. Other cases on the SWAP watch list are more advanced; the lesson does not transfer mechanically.
