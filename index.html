PARTIAL NK Launch Cadence Test: Sub-Launched System by April 30 (filed April 19)

The April 19 claim: North Korea’s cadence of four launches in 12 days would yield a fifth by April 30; if it included a sub-launched platform, it would confirm that Pyongyang was exploiting the Iran distraction to test capabilities they could not otherwise. INDOPACOM called it a “departure from normal testing rhythm.”

What happened: USNI News confirmed a second cluster-warhead Hwasong-11 test on April 24, six days after the first. The cadence claim holds. No sub-launched system materialized by today’s deadline. The diagnostic was range and platform; the second test repeated the same Hwasong-11 at the same short range. That puts this closer to capability validation on the Korean Peninsula target set than distraction exploitation.

Lesson: The cadence signal was real and was confirmed. The sub-launch upgrade test was the speculative element. Two cluster-warhead tests in six days is doctrine development, not a showcase; the third test will clarify the target-set ambition more than this one did.

War on the Rocks
Correcting Course in the Indo-Pacific
The piece examines whether the Iran war’s resource demands and strategic distraction have created an irreversible gap in Indo-Pacific deterrence posture, arguing that the administration needs a deliberate rebalancing rather than assuming the Iran and Indo-Pacific theaters can be managed in parallel at current operational tempo. It addresses munitions depletion, forward-presence signaling, and the alliance credibility question that China is watching most closely.
SWAP’s Take

The piece’s framing is honest about the trade-off the administration has been unwilling to name publicly: every Tomahawk expended at Hormuz is a Tomahawk not available in the South China Sea. The argument is structurally sound. What the piece understates is the Chinese perspective on Iran as a data-collection gift: 62 days of live-fire U.S. blockade enforcement is providing PLA Navy planners with exactly the operational intelligence they need for Taiwan Strait contingency planning, covering ROE, enforcement-zone mechanics, and how U.S.-flag and allied-flag commercial shipping behaves under naval pressure. The “deterrence gap” framing treats the problem as resource allocation. The more precise framing is that China is learning from the gap while the administration fills it.

MWI at West Point
Hitting Where It Hurts: Deep Strikes, Oil Infrastructure, and Kyiv’s Theory of Victory
The piece examines Ukraine’s sustained campaign against Russian oil processing infrastructure, analyzing whether targeting energy production imposes the economic cost on Moscow that prior infrastructure targeting (power grid, logistics) failed to achieve. It distinguishes between the political-economic logic of refinery strikes and the precision-dependent theory of victory that Kyiv is attempting to validate under severe munitions constraints.
SWAP’s Take

This piece arrives on the same day that environmental disaster fears from Russian refinery strikes are dominating Al Jazeera coverage of Ukraine. That timing is analytically useful: the theory of victory in oil infrastructure targeting requires inflicting economic pain faster than the targeting produces international sympathy for the target. The Battle of Britain analogy is worth invoking carefully here. German bombing of British industrial targets steeled British public resolve rather than breaking it. The question for Ukraine’s refinery campaign is whether Russian public opinion operates differently under authoritarian suppression, and whether oil revenue disruption feeds through to war-sustaining capacity quickly enough to matter. The piece is honest that the jury is still out on both conditions.

Foreign Policy
The Eulogy That Explains Israel’s Current Tragedy
The piece uses the rediscovered Moshe Dayan eulogy from 1956 as a lens on the current Israeli strategic condition: a state that has always known it would have to live next to its enemies but has spent the Iran war period operating as if existential military dominance is a permanent settlement. Source may be paywalled.
SWAP’s Take

The Dayan eulogy is one of the most honest strategic statements any Israeli leader ever made publicly, and the piece is right that it has been inconvenient to remember. The argument lands best in its diagnosis: Israel has conflated security with dominance in a way that Dayan explicitly warned against. Where it thins is the prescription gap. The piece demonstrates the problem clearly but does not engage seriously with the absence of a viable political interlocutor on the Palestinian side, the Gulf normalization dynamic that has been permanently altered by the Iran war, or the domestic Israeli political constraints that would make any “live with neighbors” strategic reset impossible under the current coalition. Cite it for the historical grounding; build your own prescription.

Sabotage from Afar: How Undeclared Drone Armies Prolong War and Derail Peace
The piece examines how autonomous drone systems operated by state and non-state actors below the threshold of declared conflict are becoming the primary tool for sabotaging diplomatic progress. Case studies include IRGC maritime drones, Houthi Red Sea campaigns, and Ukrainian commercial-drone-converted battlefield systems. The central argument: undeclared drone wars are structurally incompatible with ceasefire architecture because attribution is deniable and enforcement is impossible at the unit level.
SWAP’s Take

The timing of this piece is good. The IRGC’s continued harassment of commercial shipping in Hormuz during the ceasefire is exactly the pattern the piece describes: deniable, below-threshold, structurally destructive to the diplomatic track but not independently casus belli for resuming strikes. The piece’s analytical contribution is framing this not as a tactical problem (attribution, ROE) but as an architectural one: modern ceasefire agreements were designed before drones made below-threshold coercion scalable and sustainable. The implication for Hormuz specifically is that IRGC drone harassment can continue indefinitely during any negotiated ceasefire unless the agreement explicitly addresses enforcement mechanisms for exactly this category of action.

Small Wars Journal
Why the Strong Lose and the Weak Become Strong
The piece revisits the asymmetric warfare literature in the context of the Iran war, arguing that the Iran campaign is validating the structural conditions under which conventionally weaker states can impose costs disproportionate to their military capacity. The key variables examined are domestic political will asymmetry, the time horizon differential, and the role of international legitimacy in converting military defeat into political survival.
SWAP’s Take

The piece is analytically well-grounded in the Arreguín-Toft tradition, and the Iran application is apt: the regime’s survival at day 62, even in degraded form, is itself a form of strategic success if the relevant audience is domestic Iranian legitimacy rather than military capability. But the piece’s framework has a blind spot. The “weak becomes strong” thesis requires the weak party to survive long enough for the strong party’s political will to erode. The IRGC’s economic fragmentation and the succession crisis create a different variable: a regime that can win the asymmetric war in theory but may not survive the peace. Iran’s leadership problem is not that it cannot outlast the blockade; it is that outlasting the blockade may not produce a regime capable of governing the aftermath.

War on the Rocks
Rethinking Corporate Risk and Alignment in an Era of Economic Statecraft
The piece argues that corporations operating in the current era of simultaneous tariff pressure, sanctions proliferation, and state-directed industrial policy face a fundamental realignment of what “risk management” means. The traditional model of political risk as an external shock to be hedged is being replaced by political risk as an operating environment that must be strategically aligned with. Companies that treat Hormuz oil disruption or China rare-earth controls as temporary shocks are mis-modeling the decade.
SWAP’s Take

The piece’s central insight, that corporations must now pick lanes in geopolitical competition rather than remain neutral, is correct. What it understates is the compounding effect: the Hengli refinery sanctions announced this week penalized a Chinese company for buying Iranian oil, which is exactly the kind of secondary-sanctions pressure that forces third-country companies to choose between U.S. market access and other supplier relationships. The SCMP piece on minerals diplomacy, published today, describes the other side of the same phenomenon. The era of economic statecraft is not a policy choice; it is the competitive environment. The piece is worth assigning in any senior PME curriculum that still treats economics and security as separate domains.

LONG-FORM · Lawfare
China’s Legal Warfare Against Taiwan
A long-form analysis of how the PRC is steadily building an international-law and domestic-law architecture designed to characterize a Taiwan operation as a domestic enforcement action rather than a use of force across an international border. The piece walks through the PRC Coast Guard regulatory regime, the maritime line drawing, and the way Beijing has framed customs and quarantine authorities to claim peacetime jurisdiction over Taiwan’s approaches. The argument: deterrence of a Taiwan contingency cannot be just military; it must include legal preparation that makes the PRC’s peacetime jurisdictional claims contestable in international forums.
SWAP’s Take

The piece is at its strongest where most U.S. analysis is weakest: the gap between “deterrence” as Pentagon planners use the word and “deterrence” as international lawyers use it. The PRC has spent two decades building the legal scaffolding for an operation Washington still treats as a kinetic problem. Reading this against the SCMP carrier-shortfall analysis from today is illuminating: the U.S. is short on hulls and long on rhetoric while Beijing is long on law and patient on hulls. The piece’s prescriptions thin where it leans on multilateral legal pushback as if the WTO and ITLOS are still the venues that decide jurisdictional contests; that era is mostly behind us. Cite for the diagnosis. The prescription needs more on what unilateral and minilateral legal architecture (AUKUS, Quad) can actually do, and within what timelines.

LONG-FORM · War on the Rocks
What a Post-Orbán Hungary Means for Hungarians and Europe
A substantive essay examining the political-economic transition currently underway in Hungary as the post-Orbán governing coalition tries to reverse a decade of institutional capture, EU funding freezes, and judiciary reforms. The piece traces the practical mechanics of what reversing illiberalism looks like at the level of public administration, judicial appointments, and media-ownership reconfiguration. It also examines why Hungary’s case is structurally easier than Poland’s 2023 democratic correction (smaller economy, less entrenched party machine) and why it is still difficult.
SWAP’s Take

The piece pairs naturally with the SWAP Hungary special edition from April 15. The argument is honest about the asymmetry between dismantling illiberal architecture and rebuilding the institutions it captured. What the essay underweights is the Orbán-residual problem: even when an illiberal leader exits, the patronage networks, business alignments, and constitutional changes survive. Poland after 2023 is the relevant case study, not the model; Tusk’s government discovered that judicial reversal is slow even when politically backed. Hungary will discover the same. The European implication is the more important point the piece makes too quietly: a post-Orbán Budapest cooperating with Brussels removes one of the two veto players (with Slovakia) on EU-wide Russia and China policy. That is a bigger geopolitical shift than the domestic Hungarian story alone.

LONG-FORM · Small Wars Journal
The Last Interlocutor: Imran Khan, Pakistan’s Leadership Vacuum, and the Search for a Regional Mediator
A long-form examination of why Pakistan’s mediation drive between Washington and Tehran stalled in late April, focused on the structural argument that Islamabad’s civilian leadership lacks the domestic political base to credibly speak for Pakistan abroad. The piece argues Khan’s legacy and ongoing detention have left the civilian side unable to converge with the army on a unified diplomatic line, while the Iranian and U.S. sides require a counterpart that can deliver. The result: Pakistan can host but cannot broker.
SWAP’s Take

This is the deepest available account of why the Islamabad proximity-talks architecture failed to produce a face-saving statement; SWAP’s April 25 claim that the choreography would yield “mutual restraint” language got bust precisely on this dynamic. The piece is right that mediation requires not just a venue but a credible domestic interlocutor on the host side. Where it overreaches: the “Pakistan as last interlocutor” framing implies a unique role that Oman, Qatar, or Switzerland could in principle fill. The actual constraint is not Pakistan’s civil-military gap; it is that the U.S. has no domestic political need to close on Iran terms. No amount of host competence solves a principal who does not want to land. The structural insight on Pakistan is correct; the strategic implication is overstated.

LONG-FORM · Atlantic Council
Mythos, Not the Iran War, Is the Most Significant Geopolitical Warning of Our Time
A long-form essay arguing the Iran war, while the headline event, is a symptom rather than the underlying disorder; the deeper warning is the collapse of shared mythos in great-power politics. The author traces the post-1945 international order to a set of widely accepted founding stories (the lessons of Munich, the moral architecture of nonproliferation, the credibility of alliance commitments) and argues those stories have eroded faster than the institutions that depended on them. The result is a world where states transact without shared meaning, and where deterrence calibrated to the old mythos misses on the new one.
SWAP’s Take

The thesis is uncomfortable for practitioners, which is usually a sign it deserves serious engagement. Where the piece earns its conclusion: the SIPRI numbers reported this week, $2.887 trillion in global spending and Europe at 1953 rearmament cadence, are not the action of a system operating on the old mythos. They are a system that has lost confidence in shared deterrence stories and is hedging through national capability. Where the piece reaches: the prescription that policy elites must “rebuild mythos” treats stories as artifacts to be authored. They are not. They are emergent properties of repeated successful collective action over decades. The honest reckoning is that the next mythos, whatever it is, will be built by what works in the 2030s, not what U.S. and European leaders write down in 2026. The piece is correct that practitioners are operating without the scaffolding their predecessors took for granted; the implication is that any strategy that depends on shared mythos is load-bearing on a foundation that does not exist.

LONG-FORM SIGNAL SUMMARY. Today’s long-form output converges on a single theme: practitioners are operating without the institutional scaffolding their predecessors took for granted. Lawfare reads China’s Taiwan posture as legal architecture the U.S. side is not contesting at the same level; Atlantic Council reads the Iran war as a symptom of mythos collapse rather than the warning itself; SWJ reads Pakistan’s mediation failure as a leadership-vacuum problem; WoR reads Hungary’s transition as the harder-than-it-looks reverse engineering of institutional capture. The most valuable single read is the Atlantic Council Mythos essay; it reframes what every other story this week is actually telling us.
