SWAP’s Take

This is the most analytically important piece in today’s read pile, and it’s not close. The core insight — that the energy crisis has its own timeline independent of war resolution — is one that markets, negotiators, and policymakers are actively avoiding pricing. Oil below $96 on “deal optimism” is a market betting on the political solution while ignoring the physical reconstruction timeline. The 1973 comparison the piece makes is correct but understated: in 1973, producers could turn the spigots back on because the infrastructure was intact. That is simply not the case here. The food security angle is the most underreported second-order risk in the entire Iran story: fertilizer precursor disruption doesn’t show up in food prices immediately — it appears 6-9 months after planting seasons are missed. By the time it shows up in CPI data, the political window for attributing it to the war will have closed and the human cost will already be locked in. The piece earns its conclusion. Any negotiated settlement needs a concrete reconstruction financing mechanism, not just a ceasefire announcement. Nobody in the public diplomatic conversation is discussing that.

War on the Rocks
How the War with Iran Ends
Three possible endings examined: negotiated settlement with nuclear constraints, prolonged attrition, or internal regime collapse. The piece argues the administration has defined success as Iran without nukes, navy, Hormuz leverage, or proxy networks — and that achieving all four through airpower alone has no historical precedent. The 1991 Gulf War parallel is invoked: regime survived, post-war settlement produced a generation of consequences including Saddam’s 2003 invasion and the post-invasion chaos. The Chardell-Helfont framework from Foreign Affairs is cited: half-measures after military campaigns create worse problems than the original conflict.
SWAP’s Take

The three-endings framework is clean and the 1991 parallel is the right one, but the piece is doing something more important than it acknowledges: it’s surfacing the definitional problem at the center of U.S. strategy. The administration defined four objectives without a theory connecting airpower to simultaneous achievement of all four. Destroying infrastructure doesn’t eliminate nuclear knowledge. Sinking ships doesn’t end maritime harassment by asymmetric means. Degrading proxy networks doesn’t sever the ideological supply chain that regenerates them. The 1991 lesson isn’t just “regime survival produces consequences” — it’s that failure to build a day-after plan before the shooting stops is how “victory” becomes the predecessor to the next war. The administration appears to be repeating that error in real time. Ask: what does the day-after plan for Iran actually require, and is anyone in the interagency building it? The War on the Rocks piece implies the answer is no. That implication deserves a direct answer.

CSIS
Iran’s Real War Is Against the Global Economy
CSIS argues that Iran’s strategic logic in closing Hormuz was always economic coercion rather than military confrontation — the strait is the lever, not the battlefield. Closing it maximizes Iran’s coercive leverage against the international community by creating pain for parties (India, China, Japan, South Korea, Europe) that have no role in the military conflict and can’t translate their economic pain into direct pressure on the U.S.-Iran negotiating table. SWAP’s Take

The CSIS framing is analytically correct but undersells its own finding. Iran closing Hormuz isn’t just economic coercion — it’s a demonstration that a country with a severely degraded conventional military can still impose strategic costs on the global system by controlling a geographic chokepoint. The Ukraine parallel extends this: just as Ukraine’s naval drones removed Russia’s Black Sea fleet from the strategic equation despite Russian air dominance, Iran’s mines, drones, and IRGC vessels closed the world’s most important waterway despite losing 90% of its conventional navy. You don’t need a fleet to close a strait. The doctrine implication for contested maritime scenarios involving the Taiwan Strait, South China Sea, and Malacca is direct and immediate — and the CSIS piece gestures at it but stops short of the full implication. That gap is where the most important planning work needs to happen.
