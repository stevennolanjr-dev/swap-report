SWAP'S TAKES — DEFENSE
Three generals in one week is a lot to process in the middle of a campaign. The institutional question is whether the officers below them will self-censor on the lessons coming out of Hormuz — because the lessons matter more than any individual career, and civil-military candor is the mechanism by which those lessons travel up the chain. If candor degrades, the next conflict inherits the gap. That is a structural cost that outlasts any single administration.
The Hormuz-open / ports-closed framing is legally clean and operationally sustainable — but only as long as no one tests it. A Chinese-flag tanker calling at an Iranian port while Iran calls the strait open is the test case that will write the precedent. Whoever enforces first owns the framework.
The F/A-XX fight needs advocacy from the service, not just from think tanks. A Navy that cannot make its own case for sixth-gen survivability in an FY27 markup will not be able to make it in FY28 either. If the program hits FY29 with the current ratio intact, the service has effectively exited competitive strike aviation — not because of a policy decision, but because of a funding profile that nobody ever formally debated.
Sumy is the indicator for everything else in Europe. If Russia commits reserves in the next two weeks, every calculation downstream — NATO credibility, BCT return timing, Ukrainian aid disbursement — shifts. ISW's cautious language is correct for now; the real signal is reserve movement, not drone intensity.

Chatham House / RUSI: Hormuz blockade mechanics are now the reference case for distant-blockade operations
CHATHAM HOUSE / RUSI Chatham House / RUSI analysis · April 16-17
Two separate think-tank publications converged on the same observation: the Iranian-port blockade is the first major operational test of modern distant-blockade mechanics since the 1990–91 Gulf maritime interception operations. The legal framework (UNCLOS, customary law, the port-versus-strait distinction), the ROE (boarding authority, non-kinetic preference), and the compliance patterns (Chinese-flag behavior, neutral-state reactions) are generating a precedent library that will inform Taiwan Strait contingency planning on both sides. SWAP'S TAKES — STRATEGIC
Pakistan as venue is the tell. When both sides agree on a third-country host that has real equities with each of them, they have already agreed on the deal's structure — they are negotiating the announcement, not the substance. The April 22 expiry is the deadline for the announcement, not the deadline for the deal. If no announcement lands by the 22nd, the deal was never there.
The China-squeezing-Iran story is now six days old and has not reverted. That matters. Beijing's patronage of Tehran is conditional on the cost to Chinese GDP staying below some invisible threshold. The threshold was crossed around Day 2 of the blockade. Chinese pressure on Iran is now a stable fact in the negotiating environment. The Iranian hardliner camp is adjusting to it in real time.
The 1990–91 Gulf blockade precedent is doing more work in current analysis than most observers recognize. Every major think-tank paper this week cited it. That convergence is itself a signal: the reference class is stable, the policy community has settled on the frame. Which means the lessons being applied — to Taiwan, to any future great-power maritime contest — are being locked in during a hot war, not during peacetime reflection. Precedents set under fire have unusually long half-lives.
Russia's Sumy tempo is the question mark under all of this. If Moscow judges that the U.S. cannot simultaneously run a blockade, extend a ceasefire, reconstitute a European force posture, and respond to a new Ukrainian offensive, the incentive to test that judgment is non-trivial. The window is now until the BCTs return. Whether Moscow uses it depends on whether the reserve decision is made at the General Staff or at the Kremlin.

SWAP'S TAKES — DOMESTIC
The constitutional moment on war powers is happening in plain sight and will be unremarked upon in the moment. The rule being written — that executive blockade authority does not require Congressional authorization — is a bigger change in the separation of powers than any Supreme Court decision this term. Its absence from public debate is the feature, not a bug. War-authority shifts do not come with announcements.
Sunday's tariff portal is the sleeper test of the year. If it functions, executive trade policy becomes materially more flexible than it has been since the 1930s. If it is enjoined quickly, the administration loses a tool it is counting on to manage reciprocity with trading partners who are responding to the Iran disruption. Watch April 20–24 for the first test.
A 3.3% CPI print that markets absorbed without repricing is unusual. The Fed is betting the energy shock is transitory; the equity market is betting the Fed is right. Either bet requires April 22 to produce de-escalation. If it doesn't, both bets unwind simultaneously.
"Three generals, no hearings, Day 50 of a war" is a sentence that would have dominated coverage in any previous decade. It has been absorbed into the baseline noise of continuous crisis. That absorption is itself a structural change in how civil-military accountability functions.
