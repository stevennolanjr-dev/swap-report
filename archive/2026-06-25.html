Bottom Line Up Front
  • The White House asked Congress for an $87.6 billion supplemental, roughly $67 billion of it for defense, most of it to pay for the Iran war and refill what the war emptied. The request lands days after the Senate voted to halt that war, so Congress is being asked to fund a campaign it just formally objected to. The number is the war's true cost made legible: not the strikes, the restock. Around $26 billion of the munitions bill alone, by one think-tank accounting, went to the precision weapons fired in a matter of days.
  • Trump met the munitions makers, and that meeting is the real strategy document. The president sat with weapons manufacturers to push a replenishment surge for stockpiles the Gulf campaign ran down. The signal is a defense-industrial base that cannot reload at the pace a short, intense war demands, the exact vulnerability a longer Indo-Pacific fight would expose faster. The supplemental funds the symptom; the production-capacity problem is the disease.
  • Two executive orders reframed the next arms race as a quantum one. The Executive Office published "Ushering in the Next Frontier of Quantum Innovation" and "Securing the Nation Against Advanced Cryptographic Attacks," the second a post-quantum cryptography push to harden federal systems before a future quantum computer can break today's encryption. The Pentagon's own quantum strategy, separately, is being called "a first step." The race is being declared before the machine that justifies it exists, which is the point: you cannot harden after the fact.
  • RIMPAC opened in Hawaii with an emphasis on experimentation, while four NATO navies sounded the alarm over Chinese patrols off eastern Taiwan. The world's largest naval exercise convenes as the US, UK, France, and Germany jointly flag PLA activity east of the island, a sharper, more multilateral protest than usual. The Indo-Pacific did not pause for the Gulf. It used the window.
  • The settlement's diplomacy hardened into routine: Rubio reassures the Gulf, Bessent defends the deal, Hormuz crews still wait for evacuation. The Secretary of State works the Gulf capitals, the Treasury secretary defends the sanctions terms at home, and the maritime cleanup grinds on. The shooting phase is over; the argument over who got what is now the entire war, conducted in supplemental requests, reassurance tours, and the slow business of getting stranded ships out of the strait.
The Through-Line

Today the war stops being an event and becomes an accounting. The $87.6 billion request, the munitions-makers meeting, the Foreign Affairs verdict that "Iran didn't win the war," the quantum orders bracing for the next one: every lead item is about cost, capacity, and what comes after, not about combat. That is what war termination actually looks like from the inside. The fighting ends and the institutions start counting, and the counting reveals the real condition of the force. What it reveals here is a defense-industrial base that emptied its magazines against a single regional adversary and now needs an emergency appropriation to refill them, at the same moment RIMPAC and four allied navies are pointing at the Pacific. The Senate's rebuke and the supplemental are the same story told twice: a system trying to take the measure of a war its executive started, and not liking the math.

How It Aged

Aged Well June 24 call: "A $26 billion munitions burn against a regional adversary is a flashing readiness warning for any Indo-Pacific contingency." Verdict: confirmed within 24 hours. On June 25 the White House sent Congress an $87.6 billion supplemental built largely around munitions and Iran-war costs, and the president met weapons manufacturers to push replenishment, exactly the reload scramble yesterday's brief said the campaign's cost foreshadowed. Lesson: when the strike phase ends, watch the appropriations, not the headlines. The bill for the bombs always arrives after the bombs, and it tells you what the war actually cost the force, not just the target.

The Bill Comes Due
SupplementalIran War
White House sends Congress an $87.6B supplemental, $67B of it for defense
Breaking Defense / Military Times · cross-corroborated
The administration formally requested $87.6 billion in supplemental spending, with roughly $67 billion directed to defense and the bulk tied to the Iran war and the replenishment it forced. The request reaches a Congress that days earlier passed a war-powers resolution against the same conflict. The take: the supplemental is the war's invoice, and its size is the argument. You cannot vote to halt a war and then quietly refuse to pay for the magazines it emptied; the appropriation forces the legislature to own the cost of the campaign it just rebuked. Watch whether the four Republican crossover votes hold when the bill is money rather than message.
Industrial BaseMunitions
Trump meets munitions makers amid a push to replenish weapons stockpiles
Military Times / Air Force Times · cross-corroborated
The president met with munitions manufacturers to press a surge in production aimed at refilling stockpiles drawn down by the Gulf campaign. The meeting pairs with the supplemental as the demand and supply sides of the same problem. The take: the meeting is more revealing than the appropriation. Money can be voted in a day; a precision-munitions production line takes years to scale and cannot be surged on command. A force that emptied its magazines against one regional adversary and needs an emergency reload is telling you, in public, that its industrial base is not sized for the war it keeps planning to deter in the Pacific.
BudgetEpic Fury
Supplemental would give the Pentagon billions for Epic Fury, munitions, and Space Force programs
Air & Space Forces Magazine · single source
The defense share of the supplemental breaks out across Operation Epic Fury costs, munitions replenishment, and Space Force programs, a line-item picture of where a short, intense air campaign actually spends. The take: the breakout is the lesson plan. Epic Fury's accounting shows the modern shape of cost, exquisite munitions consumed fast, space and targeting infrastructure underneath, and a resupply tail that runs longer than the war. Read the appropriation as a readiness document: it maps precisely the capabilities the next contingency would burn through first.
Indo-Pacific Tempo
RIMPACPacific
RIMPAC opens in Hawaii with a focus on experimentation
Defense One / Stars and Stripes · cross-corroborated
The Rim of the Pacific exercise, the world's largest maritime drill, began in Hawaii with warships from many nations and an explicit emphasis on experimenting with new concepts and unmanned systems rather than rehearsing fixed plays. The take: RIMPAC convening while the Gulf cleanup is still underway is the Pacific's quiet answer to the bandwidth question. The experimentation focus matters more than the hull count: this is where the joint force tries to learn the distributed, unmanned, attrition-tolerant way of fighting that the Iran munitions burn just argued it needs, and to do it with allies in the room.
PRCTaiwan Strait
US, UK, France, and Germany raise alarm about Chinese patrols off eastern Taiwan
Military Times · single source, multilateral
Four major NATO members jointly flagged Chinese military patrols operating east of Taiwan, the side of the island that faces the open Pacific and the US approaches, rather than the strait. The coordinated protest is sharper than the routine single-nation statement. The take: the location is the signal. Activity east of Taiwan is about cutting the island off from relief and complicating any US response from Guam and Hawaii, not about the strait crossing everyone watches. That three European navies signed the same protest is the more durable development; it means the Indo-Pacific is no longer a problem Washington flags alone.
DPRKNaval

USNI News · single source, tracking
North Korea formally commissioned Choe Hyon, the lead ship of a new destroyer class and the largest surface combatant Pyongyang has fielded, the hardware behind Kim's recently announced nuclear-navy ambition. The take: the hull is real even if the nuclear-arming claim around it is years off. A first-in-class destroyer means a building program, not a one-off prestige ship, and it converts the nuclear-navy announcement from rhetoric into a production line worth watching. Pyongyang is methodically adding a maritime dimension to a deterrent that used to be land-only.
TaiwanAsymmetric
Taiwan budget calls for $6.6B for attack drones and unmanned surface vessels
USNI News · single source
Taipei's defense budget earmarks $6.6 billion for attack drones and unmanned surface vessels, a deliberate tilt toward the cheap, numerous, attrition-tolerant systems that the Ukraine and Gulf campaigns have validated. The take: this is Taiwan reading the same lesson the US munitions scramble teaches and acting on it faster. An island that cannot win a hull-for-hull race is buying the porcupine instead: mass over exquisiteness, denial over parity. It is the most strategically coherent line in any regional defense budget this week, and it points at the same future RIMPAC is experimenting toward.
Force, Systems, and the Building
QuantumDoD
Pentagon's quantum strategy is "a first step," CIO says
Defense One · single source
The Defense Department's chief information officer framed the Pentagon's new quantum strategy as an opening move, sketching how the department intends to prepare for both the threat of quantum-enabled codebreaking and the promise of quantum sensing and computing. The framing lands the same day as two White House quantum executive orders (see Domestic). The take: "first step" is honest and slightly alarming. The defining feature of the quantum threat is that it is retroactive; adversaries can harvest encrypted data now and decrypt it later once the machine exists. A first step taken late is still a race run from behind. The orders and the strategy together are an attempt to start hardening before the clock everyone can see actually strikes.
PRCStealth
China studied US stealth aircraft and learned the wrong lessons, analysis argues
Military Times · analysis
An assessment argues Beijing drew the wrong conclusions from a close study of US stealth programs, optimizing for the visible signatures of fifth-generation airpower while missing the systems-of-systems integration that makes American stealth actually work. The take: imitation that copies the airframe and misses the architecture is a recurring great-power error, and a fragile basis for confidence. If the analysis holds, China has built impressive-looking aircraft inside a kill chain that does not yet knit them together the way US doctrine assumes. Useful to flag, dangerous to over-believe; underestimating a competitor's ability to learn is its own wrong lesson.
USAFForward Basing
US Navy presence in Europe to get a boost after months of Iran operations
Stars and Stripes · single source, tracking
With the Gulf campaign winding down, reporting indicates the US Navy will rebuild its European presence after months of having assets pulled toward Iran operations, a reset of the posture the war drew down. The take: this is the bandwidth problem resolving in slow motion. Every carrier and escort that surged to the Gulf came from somewhere, and Europe felt the thinning while Russia pressed. The rebalancing back is welcome and revealing: it confirms the force cannot be strong everywhere at once and has to physically shuttle presence between theaters as crises rotate. The shuttle itself is the strategic constraint.
The Gulf Settlement
US-IranVerdict
Iran Didn't Win the War
Foreign Affairs · analysis
The argument: despite the narrative of survival, Iran emerged from the war strategically weaker, its nuclear program degraded, its deterrence punctured, and its leverage over the settlement narrower than its rhetoric suggests. The piece pushes against the "Tehran outlasted the bombs" reading taking hold. The take: the question of who won is the question that sets the next decade's posture, and it is genuinely contested. If Iran is weaker than it sounds, the settlement is a US position of strength to be consolidated; if it merely absorbed the blow and kept the program's bones, the deal is a pause. The inspections fight is where that ambiguity gets resolved, which is why both capitals are guarding it so hard.
HormuzDiplomacy
Rubio's reassurance mission to Gulf allies
Hudson Institute / France 24 · cross-corroborated
Secretary of State Rubio continues touring Gulf capitals to steady partners unsettled by the settlement's terms, insisting the Strait of Hormuz stays toll-free and the regional security framework holds. The take: the Gulf monarchies' anxiety is not that the deal collapses but that it succeeds on terms legitimizing Iranian leverage over the chokepoint they all depend on. Reassurance tours are a tell; you do not send the secretary of state to soothe allies who feel secure. The substance Rubio is selling is a promise about the strait that geography, not Washington, ultimately enforces.
HormuzSea Lines
A plan to evacuate hundreds of ships still stranded from the Hormuz closure is coalescing
The War Zone · single source, tracking
A coordinated plan is taking shape to move the hundreds of vessels and thousands of crew still stranded in and around Hormuz after the wartime closure, the slow logistical aftermath of the strait's shutdown. The take: the cleanup is the war's longest tail and its least visible cost. Long after the strikes stop, merchant crews sit in a strait that no one is shooting in but no one has fully reopened either. The evacuation's pace is a real-time gauge of how genuinely the settlement is holding; ships move when the parties trust the truce enough to stop using the chokepoint as leverage.
Europe and the East

Hudson Institute / Le Monde · cross-corroborated
Ukrainian long-range strikes on Russian refineries are triggering fuel shortages and visible domestic discontent inside Russia, per situation-report tracking and Le Monde reporting, an extension of the drone-and-deep-strike campaign that has been cratering airfields and cutting Crimea's logistics. The take: Ukraine is fighting the war on Russia's balance sheet. Each refinery hit converts a battlefield Kyiv cannot win on tonnage into an economic and political problem the Kremlin has to manage at home. The strategically interesting metric is no longer territory; it is Russian fuel queues and the patience of a population that was promised the war would not touch them.

The Alliance

Can Rutte sweet-talk Trump enough to soothe NATO tensions?

As a rebalancing NATO gathers in Ankara ahead of the summit, Secretary General Rutte is cast as the alliance's "Trump whisperer," tasked with managing a president fresh off a Senate rebuke over the Iran war he wants allies to help fund. IranRussia
A simmering US-Iran conflict is Moscow's ideal outcome
Carnegie Endowment · analysis
Carnegie argues a frozen-but-unresolved US-Iran standoff suits Russia best: hot enough to keep Washington's attention and resources tied to the Gulf, cold enough to avoid a regional war that would disrupt Moscow's own energy and arms interests. The take: this is the hidden beneficiary of the settlement's ambiguity. Every month the inspections fight and the Hormuz toll dispute keep the US half-engaged in the Middle East is a month its attention is not on Ukraine or the Pacific. Moscow does not need Iran to win; it needs the dispute to persist. The unresolved middle is not a failure of the deal for everyone, it is the optimal state for the party not at the table.
Executive Actions
Executive Order: Quantum Innovation
The Executive Office published "Ushering in the Next Frontier of Quantum Innovation," an executive order directing a federal push on quantum computing, sensing, and networking research and the workforce and supply chains behind them. It positions quantum as the next strategic-technology contest alongside artificial intelligence. Source: Federal Register, Presidential Documents, June 25.
Executive Order: Post-Quantum Cryptography
A companion order, "Securing the Nation Against Advanced Cryptographic Attacks," directs the migration of federal systems toward post-quantum cryptography to protect against a future quantum computer capable of breaking current encryption. The defensive order is the more consequential of the pair: it concedes that adversaries may already be harvesting encrypted data to decrypt later, and races to re-armor federal communications before that day arrives. It reads directly against the Pentagon's "first step" quantum strategy covered in the Defense tab. Source: Federal Register, Presidential Documents, June 25.

Long Reads on the War
IranStrategy
Iran Didn't Win the War
Foreign Affairs · long read
The argument: the prevailing "Iran survived, therefore Iran won" reading misreads the war's actual balance sheet. The nuclear program was set back, the deterrent was shown to be penetrable, and the regional proxy network is weaker than before, leaving Tehran negotiating from a thinner hand than its defiance implies. Why it matters: the who-won question is not academic; it sets whether the US treats the settlement as leverage to press or a pause to manage. Read it against the Hudson pieces taking the opposite tack, because the gap between them is the strategic debate that will shape the next administration's Iran policy. The inspections fight is where the abstract argument becomes a concrete test.
PakistanMediation
Will Pakistan's Iran gamble pay off?
Foreign Policy · long read
The argument: Pakistan's bid to position itself as a mediator and partner in the Iran settlement is a calculated gamble to convert proximity into influence, with real upside in regional standing and real risk if the deal sours or its rivals object. Why it matters: the settlement is rearranging the region's brokers, not just its combatants. Pakistan stepping toward Tehran, with the Iranian president recently visiting Islamabad, is a second-order effect worth tracking, because the states that gain standing from a US-Iran deal are the ones whose alignment will shift if it holds. Watch the mediators; they tell you who thinks the deal is durable enough to bet on.
From the Institutions
LeadershipAI
Homo Post Machina: How AI and Decision Analytics Can Revolutionize Leader Performance
Small Wars Journal · long read
The argument: AI and decision-analytics tools can augment military leaders' judgment under uncertainty, but only if the force treats them as decision aids that sharpen human cognition rather than as oracles that replace it. The piece sketches where machine assistance helps and where it quietly erodes the judgment it claims to support. Why it matters: this is the human-in-the-loop debate moved from the targeting cell to the command tent. As the Marines push AI into operational reporting and the Pentagon stands up a quantum strategy, the harder question is not whether the tools work but whether leaders who lean on them keep the judgment to know when they are wrong. Read it as the counterweight to every "AI will speed the kill chain" headline.
UkraineFront Lines
Hope and Determination on Ukraine's Front Lines
The Bulwark · dispatch
The argument: a ground-level dispatch from the Ukrainian front finds a force exhausted but adapting, fighting a drone-saturated war of attrition with a resolve that the macro-level "stalemate" framing flattens out of view. Why it matters: the deep-strike and refinery-shortage stories in the Strategic tab describe the war from satellite altitude; this is the same war at eye level. Both are true, and planners need both. The technical campaign against Russian logistics works only because the people in the trenches hold the line that makes the strikes matter. IranCapitulation
The Whiplash of Trump's Iran Capitulation
The Atlantic · analysis
The argument: the rapid swing from maximal threats to a negotiated settlement reads, to this critic, less as strategy than as improvisation, a capitulation dressed as a deal, with allies and adversaries alike struggling to track which version of US policy is operative week to week. Why it matters: this is the sharpest version of the case against the settlement, and it deserves a hearing alongside the Foreign Affairs "Iran didn't win" read and the Hudson defenses. The disagreement is not about facts but about whether unpredictability is a tool or a tell. For a planner, the operative risk is the same either way: allies who cannot predict US policy hedge, and hedging allies are the durable cost of a deal reached by whiplash.
