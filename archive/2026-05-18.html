Strategic Analysis
LONGFORMForeign Affairs
A New Order for the Gulf
The companion to yesterday’s “Spheres by Default.” Foreign Affairs argues the post-war Gulf settlement will be brokered by Doha, Abu Dhabi, and Muscat, with Riyadh as a load-bearing partner and Washington reduced to ratifier rather than architect. The piece treats the U.S. as constrained by its own bandwidth, not as withdrawing by choice; the structural argument is that the Gulf states have built enough independent diplomatic infrastructure over the last decade to write the next order themselves.
SWAP’s Take

The two-piece sequence is the editorial signal. Foreign Affairs does not run paired structural arguments without intent; the magazine is staking out a frame and inviting other think tanks to push back from inside it rather than around it. The argument is more comfortable on the global frame than the regional one. The Gulf claim assumes Doha, Abu Dhabi, and Muscat have the political will to broker without an external security guarantor; the UAE nuclear-perimeter fire today is the inconvenient counter-evidence. Brokers do not write orders while their critical infrastructure is being targeted; security guarantors do. Read the piece as half-right: the U.S. is shifting from architect to ratifier, but the architect role does not get devolved cleanly. It gets contested.

Small Wars Journal
Beijing’s War Dividend: China’s Asymmetric Gains from the US-Israeli Campaign Against Iran
The SWJ piece is the cleanest open-source account of what Beijing has extracted from the Iran war without firing a shot. The author’s ledger: discounted Iranian crude, locked-in long-term contracts, a Belt-and-Road position in post-war reconstruction, and an information-operations win on the Global South audience. The piece does not call it inevitable, it calls it earned, and it warns the U.S. policy community against the assumption that the war ledger is closed when the kinetic phase ends.
SWAP’s Take

The argument is correct on the four-point ledger and underplays a fifth: Beijing has used the war to validate its long-running thesis that U.S. expeditionary commitment is the structural weakness of the U.S. posture. Today’s SCMP reporting on Iran’s “unprecedented” choice of Beijing envoy is the operational expression of that thesis. The piece is the right diagnosis; the implication is that the post-war China-policy debate has to treat the Iran war as Chinese strategic input, not just U.S. strategic output.

Small Wars Journal
A Memorandum Is Not a Strategy: What the Iran MoU Must Contain
The SWJ piece reads the U.S.-Iran memorandum of understanding currently in draft as a deferral mechanism rather than a settlement. The author lays out the minimum content the document would need to function as strategy: verifiable enrichment caps, a structured uranium-export pathway, a sunset architecture that does not reset the clock, and a regional security framework that names the Gulf brokers explicitly. None of those four are in the current draft per the piece.
War on the Rocks
Missiles Aren’t Strategy: Lessons From Iran for a Pacific Air War
War on the Rocks takes the Iran war as the empirical case for the Pacific air-war debate. The author argues the campaign demonstrated the limits of stand-off precision fires against a hardened, dispersed, and politically resilient target set, and that the lesson for any future Pacific contingency is that ordnance is necessary but not sufficient. The structural point: U.S. air power has spent two decades optimizing for the wrong fight and now has to retool for the right one on a budget timeline that does not support a full re-platforming.
SWAP’s Take

This is the most important defense-policy piece in the weekend cycle. The Iran war produced enough operational evidence to settle three long-running debates: the magazine-depth question, the joint-fires de-confliction question, and the political-effect question. The piece does not settle them, but it points to the evidence that should. The implication for the FY28 budget cycle is significant: the “more JASSMs” school just lost evidentiary ground, and the “more basing, more dispersal, more sustainment” school just gained it. The Pacific reorientation will be funded out of stand-off magazine-depth procurement; the debate is no longer whether, it is how fast.
