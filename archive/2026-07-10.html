Bottom Line Up Front
  • The exchange with Iran is now in its third day, and the geography keeps widening. The War Zone tracks renewed attacks stretching into a third day; the Times of Israel reports Iran firing ten missiles at Jordan after US strikes reported near the Bushehr nuclear plant. Wednesday the answer was Kuwait, Bahrain, and Qatar. Today it is Jordan, a country with no American basing quarrel to settle and a peace treaty with Israel to protect. Each new address on the target list costs Tehran something and buys it something, and the arithmetic of which is which is not obvious from Tehran.
  • Iran buried its slain supreme leader before enormous crowds in Mashhad, and that funeral is a strategic event, not a ceremonial one. The BBC reports the crowds; Deutsche Welle and the Post and Courier carry the burial. A regime that has just interred its founder-figure's successor under American bombs has both a mandate problem and a mobilization opportunity. Mourning at that scale is the most legible thing the Islamic Republic has produced in weeks, and it is the variable that will govern how much punishment the system can absorb before it either bends or hardens.
  • Tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has fallen to a near standstill, and war-risk insurance is climbing. gCaptain reports both, the BBC reports a sharp fall in oil, gas, and cargo ships using the US-backed route, and Al Jazeera reports the collapse in transit volume. The Maritime Executive reads the attacks and counterattacks as a change in strategy rather than a tempo increase. This is the real economic front of the war, and it is not visible in the flat price of crude.
  • Crude fell for a second straight session anyway, and equities rose. WTI closed at $71.64, down about one percent; Brent at $75.88, down about one percent; the S&P 500 gained 0.81 percent and the VIX sits near 16. Cato argues the global economy has bested the Iran mess so far. The tape and the tanker data cannot both be right for long, and the reconciliation is that the risk has been transferred: it now sits on shipowners, insurers, charterers, and eventually the pump, where CBS News reports gas prices already nudging higher. The barrel is calm because somebody else is holding the bag.
  • Europe is buying the thing the Ukraine war taught it to want, and it is not buying it from Washington's stock. Breaking Defense reports twelve European nations pledging fifty billion dollars over a decade for deep precision strike, with the UK committing three billion pounds over four years. The War Zone and Military Times report Germany buying ground-launched Tomahawks. Read the first as sovereignty and the second as expedience; both are the same recognition that Europe cannot deter what it cannot reach.
The Through-Line

Two ledgers are open on this war, and they are diverging. On the military ledger, everything is getting worse: a third day of strikes, US ordnance reported near a nuclear site at Bushehr, ten Iranian missiles into Jordan, a supreme leader in the ground and a nation in the streets of Mashhad. On the financial ledger, everything is getting calmer: crude down two sessions running, the VIX at 16, American equities up. It is tempting to call one of these wrong. Neither is. What has happened is that the risk has changed address. It has moved off the barrel, where it is priced globally, liquidly, and against ample spare capacity, and onto the hull, where it is priced one charter at a time by underwriters who cannot diversify away a mine in a strait. gCaptain's war-risk numbers and the near-empty transit lane are the same fact as the falling oil price, seen from the side that has to sail through it. This matters for how the war ends. A conflict whose costs land on the oil market pressures every government at once and generates diplomatic urgency; a conflict whose costs land on a few hundred shipowners and their insurers generates paperwork. The pain is real and it is concentrated, which means it is politically quiet. Tehran has spent two decades trying to make the world feel Hormuz. This week it succeeded in making the shipping industry feel Hormuz, and the world barely looked up. Watch the insurance line, not the futures curve. And watch Oman, which the Maritime Executive says has now made its most unequivocal statement yet on transits: the littoral states are the ones who will decide whether this stays an insurance problem or becomes an oil problem.

How It Aged

Held July 9 call: the brief flagged that crude had given back part of its escalation premium and ran that observation deliberately against Foreign Policy's argument that the energy market's false dawn might be over. Verdict: held, and then some. WTI and Brent both fell again on Day 133 even as Hormuz transits collapsed, and equities gained. But the call earns only a qualified green, because it was right about the wrong price. Thursday's card said the insurance and charter data, not the flat price, would tell first. It did tell, within twenty-four hours, and it told the opposite story: gCaptain has war-risk cover climbing and shipowners pulling back, and CBS News has retail gasoline moving up. So the honest verdict is that the market is not sanguine; it is segmented. Lesson: when a brief writes "the market," it should name which market. There are at least three here, and this week they disagree.

Twelve European nations pledged fifty billion dollars over a decade for long-range precision strike, with the United Kingdom leading at three billion pounds over four years, per Breaking Defense. The stated drivers are the lessons of Ukraine and the adjustment in American posture in Europe. Two readings compete. Beyond the Gulf
IraqInfluence
Iran is losing Iraq
Foreign Affairs · single source, analysis
Foreign Affairs argues Tehran's grip on Baghdad is slipping. Set that against the week's other Iran story, which is a regime demonstrating enormous mobilization capacity at a state funeral, and you get the actual strategic picture: Iran is strong at home and weakening abroad. That combination is historically the dangerous one. A state losing its forward position and retaining its internal cohesion has both the motive to act and the means to absorb the consequences. The proxy architecture was never a luxury for Tehran; it was the reason its adversaries had to fight it far from its borders. Take it away and the war comes to Iran, which is precisely where it now is.
RussiaSanctions
The future of Russian sanctions after the Hormuz crisis
RUSI · single source, analysis
RUSI works through what a Gulf supply shock does to the architecture of Russian energy sanctions, and the answer is uncomfortable. Every barrel that does not leave Hormuz raises the market value of a barrel that leaves Novorossiysk, and the enforcement coalition's willingness to police the price cap is a function of what gasoline costs at home. This is the mechanism by which a war against Iran subsidizes Russia. It is not a paradox and it is not an accident; it is the predictable second-order effect of interdicting one exporter while sanctioning another. The reported US strike on the rail corridor Russia also uses suggests somebody in Washington has noticed.

Politics and the Courts
ElectionsInstitutions
Trump pushes out remaining members of bipartisan election commission ahead of midterms
ProPublica · cross-corroborated
The remaining members of the Election Assistance Commission have been pushed out ahead of the midterms, per ProPublica, with Politico reporting the same. The EAC is a small agency with an unglamorous job: certifying voting systems, distributing federal election funds, and publishing the standards that states voluntarily adopt. Its bipartisan structure is the whole point of it, because the alternative is a single party writing the technical standards for the machines that count its own votes. Removing the commissioners four months before a federal election does not change any ballot. It changes who is standing behind the machines when somebody asks whether they can be trusted, and that question will be asked in November regardless of the answer.

The administration will petition the Supreme Court to rehear the birthright citizenship case, per The Guardian, with The Hill carrying the same. Petitions for rehearing are granted at a rate close to zero, which makes the filing a political instrument rather than a legal one. South Carolina · Defense Industrial

The federal government will bankroll a $150 million South Carolina factory to build ammunition propellant, per the Post and Courier. Propellant is the constraint nobody notices until it binds. The United States can build shell bodies faster than it can build the energetic chemistry that fires them, and that has been the rate-limiting step in artillery production since 2022. A federally financed plant in this state is munitions-base policy arriving as local economic news.

Lowcountry · Public Health

South Carolina recorded a second consecutive annual decline in overdose deaths, per the Charleston City Paper. Two years is a trend rather than a fluctuation, and it lines up with the national pattern. Naloxone distribution and fentanyl test strips get the credit in most accounts; the more sobering explanation offered in the literature is that the population most at risk has already been depleted. Both can be true.
