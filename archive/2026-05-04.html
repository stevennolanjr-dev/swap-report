Bottom Line Up Front
  • Tehran's 14-point peace proposal landed and the President rejected it on camera; Project Freedom begins this morning. CENTCOM has been ordered to "guide" stranded commercial vessels through Hormuz with destroyers, more than 100 land and sea aircraft, multi-domain unmanned platforms, and 15,000 service members. Iran's parliament National Security chair and the Khatam al-Anbiya commander have publicly committed to attacking any U.S. ship that enters the strait. The administration's posture is that the cordon is not "hostilities," so the escort mission isn't either; Tehran's posture is that the escort mission ends the ceasefire. Both cannot be right.
  • The War Powers clock expired on May 1 with no congressional action and no legal challenge. The Senate's sixth and final WPR vote failed April 30 (47-50; Collins and Paul the only Republicans crossing). Hegseth's "the ceasefire pauses the clock" theory is now operative law by default. Sen. Reed went on the record Sunday calling out the absence of "timeouts" in the statute, which is correct as a matter of law and irrelevant as a matter of which branch is enforcing it. The DOJ chief's "we are not actively at war" line on NBC is the predicate the legal theory needs.
  • OPEC+ agreed a third output hike since the closure, and oil markets stayed flat on the Project Freedom announcement. Brent held just above $111, WTI near $101. The quiet read is that traders are pricing in the gap between an escort plan announced and an escort mission executed; the loud read, which Al Jazeera ran with, is that "announcement is one thing, safe and predictable passage is another." Anglo-Eastern's CEO said the obvious part out loud: "It takes both sides to unblock, not just one."
  • An overnight mass shooting at Lake Arcadia in central Oklahoma sent at least 12 to hospitals. A youth-party gathering near Edmond drew gunfire around 9 p.m. local; Integris Baptist took nine of the wounded, Integris Edmond three. No arrests as of Sunday night. The Oklahoma context matters: a state with light gun-violence reporting infrastructure absorbed an event that in another state would have already produced an arrest and a federal-aid request.
  • Three dead and several ill aboard a transatlantic cruise ship from a suspected hantavirus outbreak; the WHO has confirmed one case. The vessel is en route from the Atlantic to Cape Verde; passengers are being held aboard while environmental sampling completes. Hantavirus has historically been a rural-rodent disease and a cruise-ship outbreak is biologically unusual; the operating theory is contaminated provisioning rather than person-to-person spread. The piece of this nobody is naming yet is liability allocation between the carrier and the port of provisioning, which will define the post-mortem.
Through-line

Three deals on the table this morning, all rejected from inside the room rather than across the table. The President rejected Tehran's 14-point proposal and chose escort instead of settlement. Iran's parliament rejected any U.S. presence in Hormuz and chose threat instead of de-escalation. OPEC+ rejected price discipline and chose a third output hike instead. Each move makes the other two harder. The administration's theory is that it can keep the cordon, run the escort mission, and call none of it war; that theory is being load-tested today by Iran, by markets, and by the Senate, with the courts on standby. Day 66 is the first day on which the war's legal architecture, naval architecture, and economic architecture are all being asked the same question simultaneously.

Deep Reads
Foreign Affairs LONG-FORM
Why the Next India-Pakistan War Will Escalate
Foreign Affairs walks through the structural reasons the next India-Pakistan crisis will not de-escalate at the rungs the 1999 Kargil and 2019 Balakot crises did. The piece's load-bearing claim is that the rung-skipping is a function of three changes: BJP coalition arithmetic that no longer rewards de-escalation, Pakistani conventional inferiority that pushes nuclear thresholds down, and the loss of the Track-II conflict-management infrastructure both sides used to rely on. The conclusion: the next crisis goes to nuclear-shadow language faster than the previous ones, and the off-ramps that worked in Kargil and Balakot are no longer in service.
SWAP’s Take

The argument is the right one and the piece earns its conclusion through the structural-change account, not through the headline. The historical analog the piece does not name explicitly is the 1965 Indo-Pakistani war, which broke through the rung-skipping mechanism the early Cold War had built between South Asian crises and the U.S.-Soviet conversation; the parallel is that the 2026 conversation no longer has Washington as a shared anchor. India does not trust the Trump administration's mediation room, Pakistan just absorbed the political cost of failing as Iran-war intermediary, and the China-Pakistan link has matured enough to substitute for the U.S.-mediated channel. The piece thins as it moves from diagnosis to prescription; the implicit prescription, that Track-II infrastructure should be rebuilt urgently, runs into the political problem that the political class on both sides treats Track-II as elite cover for capitulation. Cite for the diagnosis; the prescription requires a different essay.

Foreign Affairs LONG-FORM
Xi’s Forever Purge
Foreign Affairs catalogs the running PLA and Politburo personnel turnover under Xi and argues the rotation is no longer about consolidation; it is about a system that cannot stop purging because the purging is now the system. The piece walks through the recent Rocket Force, Strategic Support Force, and Central Military Commission removals and frames them as instability indicators rather than control indicators.
SWAP’s Take

The piece's analytical strength is its refusal to treat the purge as a binary "Xi is in control" or "Xi is in trouble" story. The honest read it builds toward is that both can be true: Xi controls the apparatus and the apparatus is degrading because the control mechanism is consuming the institutional memory required to operate it. The historical analog is the late-Brezhnev nomenklatura churn that produced the gerontocracy by 1982; the difference is that Xi is not a gerontocrat yet and the system has not had time to stabilize around an alternative governance baseline. The implication for Indo-Pacific planning is that PLA decision-making at the strategic level is more brittle than the public-posture suggests, and that signals from second-tier officials may matter more than top-line statements during a fast-moving crisis. The piece is the right place to start the conversation; the operational question for Indo-Pacific planners is which crisis-cell stays in place when the purges keep running.

Foreign Policy LONG-FORM
This Energy Crisis Is Undoing the Last Ones
Foreign Policy reaches back through the 1973 oil shock, the 1979 supply disruption, and the 1990 Gulf-war price spike to argue that the institutional architecture each crisis built (the IEA, strategic petroleum reserves, OECD consumer cooperation) is being undone by the present crisis. The piece's argument is that the cordon's design is unintentionally rolling back the post-1973 consumer-side coordination architecture and replacing it with bilateral producer-consumer deals that look more like the pre-1973 arrangement.
SWAP’s Take

The piece earns its conclusion by tracking the institutional pieces being repurposed or sidelined. The strongest move is the observation that the IEA's Strategic Stocks coordination has not been triggered in a serious way through Day 66, despite the closure being structurally analogous to a 1973-grade event. That fact is what the piece's case rests on. The historical analog the piece does not press hard enough is the 1956 Suez crisis, in which the consumer-side coordination architecture did not exist yet and the resulting dislocation produced the institutional motivation to build it. The 2026 crisis is producing the same kind of dislocation in a system that has the institutions but is not using them; the question the piece raises but does not answer is whether the institutions get rebuilt or replaced. The Atlantic Council's energy program is the right place to track that conversation. Cite this for the framing; the operational map is in CSIS's energy work.

War on the Rocks LONG-FORM
Cheap Missiles, Not Drones, Will Win the Next Air War
A practitioner piece arguing that the drone-saturation thesis (that cheap UAS will dominate the next air war) misreads the cost-exchange math. The argument: when the cost of a drone-defeating interceptor is asymmetric to the drone, the side with cheap missiles wins. The piece walks through PAC-3 cost curves, the cheap-cruise-missile architecture both Russia and Iran are running, and the resulting air-defense exhaustion math.
SWAP’s Take

The piece's strongest move is the cost-exchange framing; the weakness is the assumption that "cheap missile" is a stable category. The Iran war's empirical contribution to this debate has been to show that the cost-exchange math runs both directions: cheap one-way attack drones cost the defender millions in interceptors, and cheap cruise missiles cost the defender even more. The Cancian munitions-depletion argument is the same finding from the inventory side. The piece does not engage seriously with the production-rate question, which is where the real cost-exchange happens; missiles are cheaper per shot but slower per month than drone production lines. The honest synthesis the piece points toward is that "cheap" is a procurement-cycle question more than a unit-cost question, and the side that builds the right production base wins. Cite for the cost-exchange diagnostic; the production-base prescription is the longer paper.

CNA Corporation DEEP SOURCE
CNA: National Security Gaps in Commercial Shipping; the 118-tanker deficit
A congressionally-mandated CNA study concludes the U.S. lacks the commercial tanker capacity required to support a major war: a deficit of 118 tanker ships and 6,400 merchant mariners for sustained combat operations. The report proposes a thirty-year commercial shipbuilding plan and a U.S. Merchant Marine Reserve Force. Two Joint Staff war games modeled sealift attrition in a contested environment to support the analysis.
SWAP’s Take

This is the most consequential single document in the current force-design portfolio and it is being underread because it lands during a war that is foregrounding kinetic news. The 118-tanker deficit is a structural ceiling on the "Rapid Dominance" doctrine the Defense Secretary has been articulating; you cannot run a sustained two-theater fight without the sealift, and the sealift cannot be built in the political-cycle horizon any administration is operating on. The historical analog is the 1980 Maritime Strategy review that produced the Reagan-era 600-ship Navy build, which was a thirty-year build that took fifteen years to deliver useful capacity and never reached its end-state. Today's challenge is harder; the shipyard base is thinner, the merchant-mariner cohort is older, and the regulatory regime around U.S.-flag shipping has accumulated barriers the 1980 baseline did not have. The piece the report does not press is whether the Merchant Marine Reserve concept is realistic without the immigration architecture changes the cohort gap implies. Treat as the load-bearing input for any serious force-design conversation through the rest of the year. Single-source verified.

RAND Corporation DEEP SOURCE
RAND: Evaluating LLMs on Technical Policy Reports
A RAND research report on the limits of general-purpose LLMs in handling technical policy and military doctrine. The benchmark RAND built found general models good at summary but weak on "structural logic" in command-relationship documents, often missing conflicting authorities. The conclusion: high-stakes military decision support requires bespoke training rather than off-the-shelf models.
SWAP’s Take

The finding is correct, the benchmark is the right kind of work, and the conclusion is too narrow. RAND's research framing treats the failure mode as a training-data problem; the more honest framing is that the failure mode is a conceptual mismatch between probabilistic language modeling and the kind of legal-and-doctrinal logic that command relationships require. Bespoke training will help around the margin; it will not fix the structural mismatch. The piece does not engage with the deeper question, which is what kind of decision support actually serves a commander under operational tempo. The answer is probably hybrid systems with humans-in-the-loop on the structural-logic questions and LLMs running summary, search, and pattern-recognition. Cite this for the empirical benchmark; the architecture conversation is the next paper, and CDAO is the right venue for it.

Hudson Institute DEEP SOURCE
Hudson: China’s Rage Machine, Power or Performance?
Miles Yu argues that Beijing's recent escalation in rhetoric is performance designed to mask structural military and economic limits, and that U.S. policymakers should treat CCP signals as psychological operations rather than indicators of imminent kinetic escalation.
SWAP’s Take

The piece is a useful corrective to the "imminent war" reading and lands as the right counter-frame to the Foreign Affairs purge piece. Yu's argument runs partly on contradiction: Beijing's ability to perform escalation rhetoric while continuing to rely on the trade and shipping architecture it publicly criticizes is itself the evidence of structural constraint. The piece thins where Yu's pieces usually thin, which is the prescription side; the implication that the U.S. should treat PRC signals as psyops is right as a posture but does not engage seriously with the cases where psyops are the prologue to action. The Indo-Pacific operational planning question the piece does not answer is which signals matter; both Yu and the Foreign Affairs piece can be right simultaneously. Read both; the synthesis is the work.

CSIS DEEP SOURCE
CSIS: Strategic Landpower Dialogue, MG Lars Lervik on the High North
The Norwegian Army chief details the integration of the High North into NATO's regional defense plans following the Finnish and Swedish accessions. Lervik emphasizes the shift from "tripwire" deterrence to "deterrence by denial" in the Arctic, and Norwegian investments in long-range precision fires and integrated air defense. The Q&A passage on "the friction of the Arctic" makes the logistics-depth argument explicitly.
SWAP’s Take

Lervik's "deterrence by denial" framing is doing the work the rest of the alliance has been hesitant to do publicly. Norway has been building this capacity for fifteen years; what is new is the willingness to describe it in those terms inside a U.S. think-tank venue. The strategic context is the Hegseth Germany order: as U.S. force posture reverts to a discretionary-Europe baseline, the alliance's high-end deterrence work is being absorbed by the regional-power tier. Norway can do this; Poland is being asked to; Spain has just signed up to it. The fragile assumption is that "deterrence by denial" works without the U.S. backstop in the same way it worked with it. The Lervik framing acknowledges the friction-of-the-Arctic point honestly; the alliance still has not done the political work of acknowledging that the U.S. backstop has changed shape. CSIS is the right venue for the honest version of the conversation.
