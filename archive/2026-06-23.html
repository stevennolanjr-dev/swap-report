Bottom Line Up Front
  • The roadmap is now two incompatible stories told from the same table. Vice President Vance says Iran will "absolutely" let IAEA inspectors back and that the US will help destroy Iran's highly enriched stockpile; the same day, Iranian officials said inspections of the sites Israel and the US bombed are not planned. Washington also waived Iran oil sanctions as part of the 60-day track. Both sides are selling the deal to different audiences, and the audiences want opposite things.
  • Scarborough Shoal sharpened from patrol to confrontation. Over the weekend the Philippine guided-missile frigate BRP Diego Silang faced four Chinese warships near the disputed atoll, with both sides trading radio challenges to leave. It follows Manila's warning that Beijing placed structures on the feature it has effectively controlled since 2012. This is the gray zone hardening while US bandwidth sits in the Gulf.
  • Britain is between prime ministers again. Keir Starmer resigned, the sixth UK premier to leave in seven years, with former Manchester mayor Andy Burnham positioned to become the seventh in a decade. Reform UK's rise and brutal May council results forced the move. A core US ally enters a leadership vacuum just as NATO burden-sharing and Ukraine support are being renegotiated.
  • China is squeezing the supply chain the Pentagon depends on. Beijing is targeting US rare-earth miners and tightening export controls again as the Pentagon faces a restocking rush, and it put Alibaba and Baidu on a military-civil-fusion blacklist. The munitions math from the Iran war runs straight into a materials base that China can throttle. Markets felt it: a Big Tech-led selloff, NASDAQ down 1.3%, VIX up 14% to 19.7.
  • The slow files kept grinding. China's Liaoning carrier group wrapped a 40-day South China and Philippine Sea deployment; the US killed two men in a strike on a suspected drug boat in the Caribbean; lawmakers warned the acting DNI against a workforce purge; and a Coast Guard helicopter crashed in Alaska with four aboard. The stories that did not make the lead still set the conditions for the ones that did.
Through Line
Day one survived; day two is where the contradictions surface. The roadmap depends on a sequence wired in series, and yesterday's brief flagged Lebanon as the first hot wire. Today the strain shows somewhere subtler: verification. Vance is telling the American public that inspectors return and the enriched stockpile gets destroyed, the concrete proof a skeptical Congress demands. Tehran is telling its own hardliners that the bombed sites stay closed, the concession it cannot make and survive. Those two messages cannot both be the deal. One is spin for a domestic audience, and the 60-day clock is really a countdown to finding out which. Meanwhile the competition that does not pause for Gulf diplomacy keeps moving: four PLA hulls against one Philippine frigate, a rare-earth squeeze timed to a Pentagon restocking rush, a carrier group completing a deployment that rehearses the blockade of an island. The Clausewitzian read holds from yesterday: the war changed instruments, it did not end, and the political object, a verifiable cap on Iran's enrichment, is exactly the thing the two sides are now describing differently. Watch the verification language, not the headline numbers. That is where the deal lives or dies.

The Long Game: China, Russia, and the Next Threat
The Mirage of China's Military Edge (Foreign Affairs)
Foreign Affairs  |  June 2026  |  China Net Assessment
Dennis Blair argues the consensus that China has closed or surpassed the US military gap is overstated, that Chinese forces remain untested, command culture is brittle, and the headline hardware numbers obscure real operational weaknesses.
SWAP’s Take

This is a useful corrective to threat-inflation, and it should be read against today's news, not in isolation. Blair is right that an untested force is an unknown quantity, and that the PLA has not fought a real war since 1979. But the corrective has its own failure mode: comfort. A Liaoning group completing a 40-day deployment and four warships facing down a Philippine frigate are not hardware-count abstractions; they are reps, and reps are how an untested force becomes a tested one. The honest net assessment is not "China is a paper tiger" or "China has won"; it is that the gap is closing through exactly the sustained operations Blair correctly notes are unproven. Clausewitz's point about the difference between war on paper and real war cuts both ways: it humbles Chinese confidence, and it should humble ours about predicting how their inexperience plays out. Worth reading alongside the Scarborough item to keep the abstraction honest.

The Next Russia Threat (Foreign Affairs)
Foreign Affairs  |  June 2026  |  Russia Reconstitution
Michael Kofman argues the more dangerous Russia is the one that emerges after the Ukraine war, a force that has learned, reconstituted, and adapted under fire, and that the West risks misreading current attrition as permanent weakness.
SWAP’s Take

Kofman is the analyst to read on Russian military power, and the thesis lands because it inverts the comfortable story. The frozen front and the fuel-sales halt in Crimea, real as they are, measure today's Russia, not tomorrow's. Armies that survive a hard war often come out more lethal, the Wehrmacht after 1940, the US Army after Vietnam's hollowing and rebuilding, because combat is the most expensive education there is and Russia is paying full tuition. The strategic error the West keeps making is linear extrapolation: assuming the Russia of 2026 attrition is the Russia of 2030. This connects directly to the burden-sharing fight: if Hegseth's European drawdown assumes a permanently degraded Russia, and Kofman is right that reconstitution is the real threat, then the drawdown is timed to the wrong curve. Read this and the Germany-strategy piece together. The question both raise is whether Europe rearms faster than Russia rebuilds.

Doctrine: The Drone Debate
Drones Are Not the Future of War, They Are the Problem to Be Solved (MWI)
Modern War Institute at West Point  |  June 2026  |  Drones Doctrine
The Modern War Institute essay pushes back on drone-maximalism, arguing that cheap uncrewed systems are not a revolution to be embraced so much as a threat to be countered, and that fixating on fielding more drones misreads the actual problem.
SWAP’s Take

The contrarian framing is sharper than it first looks. The Ukraine-driven consensus, "drones change everything, buy more," skips the harder question the piece foregrounds: a weapon that is cheap for you is cheap for the enemy, so a drone-saturated battlefield advantages whoever solves the counter-drone problem, not whoever fields the most drones. That is the right lens. The Crimea strikes prove drones can impose cost; they do not prove drones win wars, because Russia is absorbing the cost and the front has not moved. The doctrinal trap is treating a leveling technology as an edge. The MWI argument maps onto the materials story too: if counter-UAS depends on interceptors and electronics that run through the same strained, China-throttled supply chain as everything else, then "solve the drone problem" runs into the same industrial-base wall as refilling the missile magazines. The future of war is not the drone; it is who can produce the counter at scale. Worth the full read for anyone building a force-design argument.

From the Institutions
The 2026-27 Annual Estimate of the Strategic Security Environment (US Army War College Press)
U.S. Army War College Press  |  June 2026  |  Strategy Industrial Base
The War College's annual estimate maps the year's strategic risks with an emphasis on defense-industrial vulnerabilities and multi-theater readiness friction, anchoring resource shortfalls to specific combatant-command requirements rather than general strategy talk. It reads the widening gap between operational plans and the logistics to sustain them as the central problem of contested-environment warfare.
SWAP’s Take

This is the document that ties the whole brief together, and it is worth reading precisely because it refuses the comfortable abstractions. Every other thread today, the munitions math behind the Iran deal, the rare-earth squeeze, the three-theater budget straining behind the new Pacific command, is a symptom of the single condition the estimate names: plans have outrun logistics. The value of an institutional baseline like this is that it converts the anecdote into a requirement, "we are short" becomes "we are short against these 105 command-sponsored requirements," which is the language that actually moves appropriations. The Clausewitzian point sits underneath it: friction is not the exception in war, it is the medium, and an industrial base that cannot replace losses turns friction into culmination. The honest read is that the United States has spent a year discovering its strategy assumed a logistics system it does not have. Whether the FY2027 NDAA closes that gap or just names it is the question the estimate frames and the appropriators will answer.
