Through-line

Yesterday's brief argued the cordon-and-escort framework would survive only if the first convoy transited unmolested. It did not. The IRGC tested the framework with fast boats, the U.S. answered with rotary-wing, and the framework's legal architecture is now downstream of an engagement that produced losses on Tehran's side. Two reciprocal moves followed inside twelve hours: Iran widened the target geometry to the UAE, and Putin announced a Victory Day truce timed to compress the Western news cycle around Moscow's parade staging. Each move is the same shape: an attempt to set the political-acoustics frame ahead of the kinetic frame. Day 67 is the day the Iran war's political-and-military pacing decoupled from the ceasefire architecture; the question is whether the new tempo stabilizes around tit-for-tat or accelerates toward a second-front fight in the Gulf.

Le Monde's reporting on the same desk confirms the junta and its Russian ally are losing ground in the north.
SWAP’s Take

The piece's diagnosis is correct and the timing is the right one. The strongest move is treating Mali as a leading indicator for the wider Sahel security architecture rather than as an isolated Bamako story. The piece thins where Foreign Policy pieces of this kind usually thin: the prescription requires Western re-engagement at a tempo and political cost that no current Western capital is prepared to absorb, and the Russian-Wagner-successor architecture is not actually the binding constraint. The binding constraint is local political-economy and the absence of a regional-power broker that can replace the French presence. The piece does not engage seriously with that architecture, which is its main weakness.
