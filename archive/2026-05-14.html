LONGFORMForeign Affairs · Structural read on China
China Was Ready for the Age of Anarchy
The most important strategic piece of the week. Foreign Affairs' argument is that the institutional, industrial, and diplomatic posture Beijing built over the past decade was designed for exactly the disorder the international system now produces, and that the U.S. side is bargaining tactically with a state that built itself strategically for this moment. The piece is unsparing about the failure-of-imagination on the U.S. side and careful about not overstating the Chinese position. LONGFORMMWI at West Point · Taiwan defense
Hellscape Defense in Taiwan: Would It Work?
MWI's careful piece on the Hellscape concept, the doctrine of saturating the Taiwan Strait with unmanned and missile-defense capability to make a Chinese amphibious operation prohibitively costly. The piece is sympathetic to the underlying logic and unsparing about the operational, industrial, and political requirements that make the doctrine real rather than rhetorical. The relevance to today's Beijing summit is structural: the Hellscape concept is the operational counterweight to a Taiwan policy that requires the deterrence math to actually close, and the U.S. industrial base required to produce the unmanned and missile inventories is the same constraint the new Pentagon missile program and affordable munitions push are trying to fix.
LONGFORMWar on the Rocks · Air and missile defense
The New Era of Air and Missile Defense
War on the Rocks' Cogs of War episode-essay on the structural shift in air and missile defense doctrine and technology. The piece walks through the cost-imposition arithmetic, the industrial base constraints, and the doctrinal integration questions that determine whether Patriot-class systems and their successors can keep up with the threat surface from Iranian, Russian, and Chinese inventories. The piece is operationally serious and politically pointed: the U.S. and allied air and missile defense capacity is on a path that does not close on current demand absent a deliberate force-design and industrial-mobilization shift.
LONGFORMThe New Yorker · Refugee resettlement
The End of Refugee Resettlement
The New Yorker's letter from Jordan on the operational collapse of the U.S. refugee resettlement program. The piece is careful with the data and pointed about the human cost of the policy retrenchment. The framing puts the resettlement decline alongside the longer arc of post-2017 refugee admission ceilings; the new development is that the operational infrastructure resettlement runs on, vetting, transportation, voluntary-agency partnerships, has now degraded to a point where reversibility is no longer a near-term question. The piece reads alongside the Atlantic's longer essay on conservative masculinism and the broader cultural moment.
Through-line. Five longreads, one structural pattern: the institutional architecture that managed the post-1989 international order is degrading on multiple axes simultaneously. The China-strategic piece names what the U.S. side has not adjusted to. The Chatham House piece names what is salvageable in nuclear order. The MWI Hellscape piece names what would have to be true for the Taiwan deterrence math to close. The War on the Rocks air-defense piece names the industrial-base constraint underneath all of it. The New Yorker piece names what the humanitarian dimension of the same retrenchment looks like on the ground.
