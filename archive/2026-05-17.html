Bottom Line Up Front
  • The USS Gerald R. Ford comes home, and the Iran War campaign closes its first surface-force chapter with a Presidential Unit Citation. The Ford strike group’s return after an 11-month deployment, reported by USNI News, Stars and Stripes, the Department of Defense, and the Charleston-market Live 5 News, ends the longest carrier surge of the modern era. Task & Purpose has the unusual angle: the strike group receives a Presidential Unit Citation for Iran War service, an award rarely given to a CSG. The political signal is the operational closeout; the order-of-battle signal is what the Navy chooses to replace the Ford with on station.
  • Ukrainian drones reach the Moscow region in record numbers, hit a refinery, kill three. SCMP and BBC both lead with the strike. The combination of scale and the refinery target moves the war out of the interdiction phase and into a deliberate strategic-bombing campaign against Russian fuel infrastructure. The political effect inside Russia is the part to watch: any sustained refinery campaign produces an oil-product squeeze, and the Kremlin has shown it will absorb battlefield casualties more readily than it absorbs gasoline lines.
  • U.S. and Nigerian forces kill an Islamic State West Africa senior leader. Task & Purpose and France 24 both confirm the kinetic action, which President Trump previewed publicly the night before. The story matters not because ISIS–WA was about to break out of the Lake Chad basin, but because it is the most visible U.S. counterterrorism kinetic on the African continent in a year, and because it pairs with the broader withdrawal-and-replace pattern (smaller footprint, more enabling, more partner kinetic). Watch whether AFRICOM moves the partner-enabling line elsewhere in the Sahel.
  • Beijing-summit commentary has converged on “nothing.” Foreign Affairs publishes “Spheres by Default,” reading the international order as quietly partitioning into regional zones of influence with no grand bargain to legitimize them. The Atlantic Council piece on Trump in Beijing reads the meeting as “respect and leverage,” not concession or coordination. The Bulwark’s flat read holds: the trip produced no Iran handle, no Taiwan signal, no chip-export movement. Friday’s coverage already absorbed the disappointment; today’s reads are the second-day think-piece tier sealing the verdict.
  • Friday tape closed risk-off; the weekend reset hasn’t arrived. The S&P 500 finished off 1.24%, the VIX +6.78% to 18.4, gold −1.25% to $4,562, BTC at $78,406 (+0.38% intraday Friday but still under the prior week’s line). WTI settled at $101.02 (−1.32%) while Brent firmed +2.20% to $109.26, a crude-spread split that is unusual outside a transit-risk episode and aligns with the Hormuz watch. The 10Y at 4.59% (+3.00%) and DXY up 0.23% to 99.3 say the bid is going to U.S. duration and the dollar, not out of risk altogether.
Through-line

The weekend reads as a closing of one Iran-War chapter and the opening of another. The Ford comes home decorated; the Navy now owes an answer on what replaces a 100,000-ton presence in or near Sixth Fleet’s operating area. Russia’s war metastasizes inward as Kyiv reaches Moscow’s skies in volume, putting refinery throughput, not soldiers, at the political center. The U.S. counterterrorism posture in Africa registers a clean partner-enabled win on the same day the post-Beijing think-piece tier (Foreign Affairs, Atlantic Council, The Bulwark, The Atlantic) closes the summit ledger at zero. Foreign Affairs’ framing of “spheres by default” is the most economical description of the order taking shape: no grand bargain, no global concert, no coalition rebuild, only regional containment lines that hold because nobody has the energy to redraw them. The Cassidy primary loss in Louisiana is the domestic echo of the same story: a senator who broke ranks on one Trump-era vote ends his career on the redistricting-and-loyalty matrix. Watch the Putin readout from Beijing, the next Ukrainian deep-strike target, the post-Ford carrier ladder, and the Louisiana GOP runoff.

Strategic Analysis
LONGFORMForeign Affairs
Spheres by Default
Foreign Affairs argues that the international order is not collapsing into open competition or coalescing around any new architecture; it is partitioning into regional spheres of influence by default, because none of the great powers has the energy, resources, or domestic license to build a new global system. The piece reads the Beijing summit’s emptiness as confirmation: a U.S.–China “deal” would have required somebody to do the architectural work, and nobody has. Russia keeps its near abroad; China keeps its first-island concerns and its Belt and Road infrastructure; the U.S. keeps the Atlantic alliance and the Pacific bilaterals; the rest of the world hedges. The piece does not call this a stable equilibrium. It calls it the geometry produced by exhaustion.
SWAP’s Take

The economy of the framing is what makes it useful. “Spheres by default” is more honest than “new Cold War,” “G2 condominium,” or “rules-based order under stress.” The point is the absence of a positive design. The risk is the same as every spheres-of-influence settlement in history: stability depends on the boundary lines being clear, and clear boundary lines require either a Yalta-equivalent moment of recognition or a war that establishes them. We are getting neither, which means the regional contests at the seams (Taiwan, the Black Sea littoral, the Gulf, the Sahel) carry more weight than the framing implies. The think piece is the diagnosis; the implication is that the next decade is decided at the seams.

Atlantic Council
Trump in Beijing: respect, leverage, and the new realism of U.S.–China relations
The Charai piece reads the summit as a recalibration of expectations rather than an outcome. The argument: both sides came to Beijing intending to demonstrate they could function in the absence of an agreement, and the meeting served that purpose. The “new realism” is the explicit acknowledgement that no grand strategic resolution is on the table and that the bilateral runs on episodic transactions. Charai treats this as a stabilization rather than a failure.
SWAP’s Take

The Charai read is the most generous available, and even it concedes the absence of substance. The Atlantic Council piece is best paired with Foreign Affairs: read together, they argue that the Beijing meeting was a managed equilibrium, not a strategic move. The risk in that reading is complacency. Managed equilibria fail at predictable seams; for U.S.–China the obvious seam is Taiwan, the less-obvious one is fentanyl, and the underrated one is the AI-export-control regime.

NPR
The foreign fighters who helped topple Assad, and why China worries about them
NPR’s piece is the cleanest open-source account to date of the Uyghur fighter cohort embedded in the Syrian opposition coalition that brought down Assad. The strategic implication is the part Beijing reads carefully: a battle-hardened, externally trained, ideologically primed Uyghur cohort with a post-Assad-Syria operating area is the closest thing to a real return-vector concern China has had in two decades.
SWAP’s Take

The story matters two ways. Domestically, it gives the Chinese Communist Party fresh political license to harden Xinjiang policy and to widen the security-services footprint already in place. Externally, it gives Beijing a counterterrorism rationale to engage in post-Assad Syria, which it has thus far approached at arm’s length. Watch whether Chinese energy diplomacy in the Levant accelerates and whether the Shanghai Cooperation Organization picks up a Syrian observer or affiliate status in the next 12 months.
