Bottom Line Up Front
  • Iran has begun public mourning for Ayatollah Khamenei, killed in February, with his body lying in state on the war's 127th day. The BBC and the Economist both carry the story, and the timing is the analysis: a regime that deferred its supreme leader's public funeral rites for five months of war is choosing to hold them now, in the middle of a negotiation whose central open question is whether anyone in Tehran can deliver on a deal. A state funeral is legitimacy work. Whoever presides over this one is telling the country, and the American delegation, who actually holds authority in the post-Khamenei system.
  • The Hormuz fee dispute went multilateral in 24 hours: a senior Iranian official warned the UK and France that "crisis-makers will be held accountable," China publicly urged "unimpeded passage" of the strait, and Japan is weighing a return to Iranian crude. Friday's brief argued the transit-fee proposal deserved more weight than the diplomatic adjectives because it was the only artifact specific enough to be rejected. The rejections have started arriving. When Beijing, whose tankers depend on the strait, and Tokyo, whose refiners want the oil, both begin negotiating the toll's terms in public, the fee plan has stopped being a trial balloon and become the de facto agenda.
  • The republic turns 250 today under a heat emergency that could make this the hottest July 4 on record in parts of the country, a national special security event posture the Secret Service calls a "heightened threat environment," and a presidential address at Mount Rushmore warning of a communist "enemy" within. The celebration itself has become a security and logistics operation: a massive military flyover over the capital, record travel volumes, and electricity prices soaring under an eastern heat dome. The anniversary's through-line writes itself; the argument the Declaration started is still being had, at volume, on its own birthday.
  • Europe has already replaced most of the US capability cuts within NATO, the alliance's top commander says, and Europeans are positioned to fill almost all remaining gaps in NATO's defense plans. Coming one day after Trump called current US support levels "ridiculous" and the US Army Europe and Africa headquarters dropped to three stars, the SACEUR statement reads as the other half of the same transaction: the American reprice is real, and so is the European backfill. The Ankara summit now convenes over a fait accompli on both sides, which may be the quietest major force-structure shift of the decade.
  • Moscow and Kyiv spent the day contesting facts rather than ground: Putin claimed the capture of Kostiantynivka and Ukraine's General Staff denied it, while Ukrainian drones hit a port and oil terminal in Russia's Leningrad region, the deepest strike package of the week. The Kostiantynivka claim matters less for the city than for the pattern; contested-capture announcements have preceded every major Russian information push of the war. The Leningrad strike matters more: Ukrainian reach now prices in refineries and export terminals on the Baltic, not just Belgorod, and Friday's Crimea shelter damage shows the same campaign working the peninsula.
The Through-Line

Today's brief is a study in ritual doing strategic work. Tehran opens a state funeral it postponed for five months, because a regime negotiating the end of a war it did not win needs the legitimacy a funeral confers, and needs the succession it implies to look settled. Washington stages the republic's 250th birthday as a national special security event, a celebration conducted from inside a perimeter, with the president using the founding anniversary to name an internal enemy. NATO prepares a summit in Ankara that will perform alliance unity over a repricing both sides have already executed; the Europeans did not wait for the communique to start backfilling. Even the Kostiantynivka exchange is ritual of a kind, the declared capture and the immediate denial, a liturgy both armies now know by heart. None of this is empty theater. Rituals are how states signal what they can no longer simply assert: continuity in Tehran, cohesion in Brussels, confidence in Washington. Clausewitz put moral forces among the most decisive elements in war, and days like this one show why; the physical facts, one strait, one city, one alliance, moved barely at all, while every government involved spent the day working on belief.

The parties' characterizations lag the mechanism by about a news cycle, and the mechanism is where the interests show.
