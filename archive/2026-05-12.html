Bottom Line Up Front
  • Trump leaves for Beijing calling Tehran's counter “garbage” and the ceasefire “on life support.” The framing closes whatever remained of the Pakistan-channel diplomatic track and puts the question of next steps squarely on the summit table. Tehran's official line, carried by France 24 from Iranian state media, is that Iranian forces are ready to respond to attacks; Iran's negotiators are publicly accusing Washington of demanding capitulation. The two positions are not converging. The Hormuz blockade is doing the talking on the U.S. side; IRGC posture statements are doing it on the Iranian side.
  • Saudi Aramco puts the price of a closed Strait of Hormuz at 100 million barrels per week. CEO Amin Nasser told France 24 the lost volume is now beyond the system's ability to absorb without redrawing global routing. Aramco's read confirms what shipping insurers have been saying for two weeks: the war is not a futures-curve event anymore, it is a physical supply event, and the redrawing is permanent if the closure extends past summer. WTI front-month sits at $101 with Brent at $107 on premarket tape.
  • The UAE confirms, for the first time officially, that it received an Iron Dome battery from Israel. Times of Israel reports a U.S. envoy on record. The disclosure lands two days after the Financial Times reported that the Iranian-hit UAE gas plant will not be fully repaired until 2027. The two stories together describe a Gulf state that has accepted Israeli air-defense integration as the price of survivable infrastructure, which is a structural realignment the Iranian regime will read as a strategic loss whatever happens at the negotiating table.
  • Russia's Victory Day truce dies overnight under 200 drones; Yermak is named in a money-laundering probe. Kyiv Independent and Al Jazeera both report the ceasefire collapse hours after its expiry, with residential buildings struck in Kyiv. Separately, Ukraine's anti-corruption agencies named Andriy Yermak, Zelenskyy's former chief of staff, as a suspect in a vast money-laundering case tied to a luxury development near the capital. The two stories arrive on the same morning the EU sanctioned Russian officials over child abductions. Putin's "war ending" rhetoric is not matched by the operational picture.
  • Senate Republicans warn Trump publicly against a Cuba military attack; primaries open in Nebraska and West Virginia. The Hill reports a bipartisan caution coalescing around the Cuba contingency that has been quietly building inside the administration for ten days. The Senate Republicans are putting on the record that they will not back a unilateral move on Havana while two simultaneous wars consume the force. Voters in Nebraska's competitive House districts go to the polls today; West Virginia's primary will surface where the GOP base sits on the war supplemental.
Through-line

The center of gravity for this week is shifting from the negotiating table to physical reality. Aramco's 100 million barrels per week is a number that survives any rhetoric; the UAE's quiet acceptance of Israeli air defense is an alignment that survives any peace deal; Russia's 200 drones across an expired truce is a campaign tempo that survives whatever Putin tells European interlocutors. The diplomatic vocabulary, "garbage," "surrender," "life support," is doing audience-management work for both Tehran and Washington while the material facts harden underneath the words. Beijing's summit gives Xi a clean read on whether the U.S. side is closer to a deal or closer to widening the war, and the U.S. side arrived without leverage it has not already spent.

SWAP’s Take

The stormwater project and the first-responder mental health summit are the two local items with the longest tail. The stormwater investment is the city making a structural commitment to operate the peninsula through more frequent flooding; that commitment compounds across decades and shapes the cost calculus for every adjacent investment. The mental health summit is the kind of professional-community knowledge exchange that institutionalizes practices that survive specific leaders and election cycles. Both are slow-moving and both are the right things to track.
